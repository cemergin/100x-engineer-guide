## 1. The Engineering Career Ladder

Every engineering organization has a career ladder, whether it is formally documented or not. Understanding the ladder is the first step to navigating it deliberately rather than hoping for the best.

### Typical Levels

| Level | Title (varies by company) | Typical Experience | Core Expectation |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| L1/L2 | Junior / Associate Engineer | 0-2 years | Complete well-defined tasks with guidance |
| L3 | Mid-level Engineer | 2-5 years | Own features, design within boundaries |
| L4 | Senior Engineer | 5-10 years | Own systems, influence team direction, mentor |
| L5 | Staff Engineer | 8-15 years | Own cross-team technical direction, define problems |
| L6 | Principal Engineer | 12-20+ years | Org-wide technical strategy, anticipate problems years ahead |
| L7 | Distinguished / Fellow | 15-25+ years | Company-wide or industry-wide impact |

> **Note:** The numbers above are rough guides. Some people reach Staff in 6 years; others are happy staying Senior for 20. Neither path is wrong.

### What Changes at Each Level

**Junior (L1/L2):** You are given well-scoped tasks. Your job is to learn the codebase, the tools, and how to ship reliable code. Success means completing tickets on time, asking good questions, and writing code that does not create extra work for others.

**Mid-level (L3):** You own entire features from design to delivery. You can take a product requirement, break it into technical tasks, and build it. You design within the boundaries of existing architecture. You start reviewing others' code and contributing to team discussions.

**Senior (L4):** You own systems, not just features. You make technical decisions that affect the whole team. You mentor junior and mid-level engineers. You are trusted to handle ambiguity — someone can give you a vague problem and you will figure out the right approach. You influence team direction through proposals, design docs, and technical leadership.

**Staff (L5):** You own cross-team technical direction. The critical shift here is that you **define problems**, not just solve them. You see a gap nobody else has articulated, write the proposal, build consensus, and drive it to completion. Your impact goes beyond your immediate team.

**Principal (L6):** You set org-wide technical strategy. You anticipate problems 1-3 years out and steer the organization to avoid them. You shape how hundreds of engineers work. Your influence is often exercised through architecture reviews, technical strategy documents, and mentoring Staff engineers.

**Distinguished / Fellow (L7):** You are recognized as a leading expert in your domain, often across the industry. You represent the company externally. You work on problems that define the company's technical future. Very few roles exist at this level.

### The Scope Dimension

The simplest way to understand the career ladder: **your impact radius grows**.

```
Junior:     Your code
Mid:        Your feature
Senior:     Your team's systems
Staff:      Multiple teams' technical direction
Principal:  Org-wide technical strategy
Fellow:     Company-wide or industry-wide influence
```

Every promotion is fundamentally about demonstrating you can operate at a wider scope.

### Time Between Levels

Typical timelines (these vary significantly by company and individual):

- **Junior to Mid:** 1-2 years
- **Mid to Senior:** 2-4 years
- **Senior to Staff:** 3-6 years (often longer)
- **Staff to Principal:** 4-8 years (if ever — very few roles)

### Senior Is a Terminal Level (and That Is Fine)

At most companies, Senior Engineer is the **terminal level** — meaning you can stay there indefinitely without any expectation to promote. This is not a consolation prize. Senior is the level where you have deep technical autonomy, strong compensation, and the freedom to focus on craft. Many of the best engineers you will ever work with are long-tenured Seniors who chose depth over breadth.

The question is not "how do I get to Staff?" — it is "what do I want my day-to-day work to look like?" If the answer is "I want to write great code, own systems, and mentor a few people," Senior is an excellent place to be.

## 2. Staff+ Engineer Archetypes

Will Larson's framework (from *Staff Engineer: Leadership beyond the management track*) identifies four archetypes of Staff+ engineers. Most Staff+ roles are a blend of these, but understanding the archetypes helps you figure out where you fit.

### The Tech Lead

**What they do:** Drive the technical direction of a specific team. They are close to the code — often writing or reviewing significant portions of it. They balance technical decisions with product and people considerations. They set technical standards for the team and ensure the team ships reliably.

**Day-to-day:** Code reviews, design discussions, sprint planning, 1:1s with engineers on the team, writing technical specs, unblocking teammates, and still writing code regularly.

**Best for you if:** You enjoy being deeply embedded in a team, you care about both the technical and human aspects of shipping software, and you want to stay close to the code while having broader influence.

### The Architect

**What they do:** Define cross-team or org-wide technical vision. They design systems that span multiple teams, ensure architectural consistency, and make technology choices that affect the whole organization. They may not write much production code day-to-day.

**Day-to-day:** Architecture reviews, writing and reviewing design documents, meeting with multiple teams to align technical direction, evaluating new technologies, creating reference architectures, and mentoring senior engineers on system design.

**Best for you if:** You think in systems, you enjoy the design phase more than the implementation phase, and you are comfortable influencing through documents and conversations rather than pull requests.

### The Solver

**What they do:** Parachute into the hardest problems the organization faces. They operate across teams as needed, going wherever the most critical technical challenge is. They unblock others by tackling problems nobody else can solve.

**Day-to-day:** Deep investigation of hard problems (performance, reliability, security), prototyping solutions, writing code in unfamiliar codebases, teaching teams how to maintain the solution after they move on.

**Best for you if:** You thrive on variety, you are energized by hard problems, you are comfortable being a stranger on a new team frequently, and you do not need to see the long-term life of the systems you work on.

### The Right Hand

**What they do:** Extend the capacity of a VP or Director of Engineering. They work on whatever matters most to the org right now — which changes frequently. They represent engineering leadership in rooms where the VP cannot be present.

**Day-to-day:** Highly variable. One week it might be leading a technical due diligence for an acquisition, the next it might be driving an org-wide migration, the next it might be debugging a political problem between two teams.

**Best for you if:** You are comfortable with ambiguity, you enjoy organizational problems as much as technical ones, and you are willing to work on whatever is most impactful rather than what is most interesting to you personally.

### Self-Assessment: Which Archetype Fits You?

Ask yourself these questions:

1. **When you have free time at work, what do you gravitate toward?** Code (Tech Lead/Solver), design docs (Architect), or organizational problems (Right Hand)?
2. **How do you feel about working on the same team for years?** Energized (Tech Lead), restless (Solver), indifferent (Architect/Right Hand)?
3. **What is your relationship with ambiguity?** You want to reduce it for your team (Tech Lead), you want to resolve it through design (Architect), you seek it out (Solver/Right Hand)?
4. **How much do you care about organizational dynamics?** Necessary evil (Solver), important context (Tech Lead/Architect), genuinely interesting (Right Hand)?

### How to Signal Which Path You Want

Do not wait for your organization to assign you an archetype. Demonstrate the behaviors of the archetype you want:

- **Tech Lead path:** Take ownership of your team's technical standards. Write the on-call playbook. Drive the design reviews. Be the person who ensures quality.
- **Architect path:** Write cross-team design docs without being asked. Propose standards that span teams. Attend architecture reviews outside your team.
- **Solver path:** Volunteer for the hairiest incidents and investigations. Help other teams when they are stuck. Build a reputation for cracking hard problems.
- **Right Hand path:** Offer to help your engineering director with special projects. Volunteer for cross-org initiatives. Show you can represent engineering leadership effectively.

## 3. IC vs Management Decision

At some point in your career, someone will ask if you have thought about management. This is one of the most consequential decisions you will make, and it deserves careful thought rather than a reflexive answer.

### Signals That Management Might Suit You

- You find yourself more energized by helping someone else solve a problem than solving it yourself
- You are frustrated by organizational problems (unclear priorities, poor communication, misaligned teams) and want to fix them
- You enjoy 1:1 conversations about people's growth and goals
- You want to set the direction for a team, not just execute on it
- You are comfortable making decisions with incomplete information
- You have already been informally leading — organizing the team, resolving conflicts, setting priorities

### Signals to Stay IC

- You lose energy in meetings and gain it when coding
- You find performance reviews and career conversations draining rather than energizing
- You want to go deep on technical problems, not wide across organizational ones
- You have a strong intrinsic drive to build things with your own hands
- You measure your best days by the quality of the code or system you produced, not by the conversations you had
- You are interested in management mostly because it seems like "the next step" (it is not — Staff Engineer exists for a reason)

### The "You Can Always Switch Back" Myth

People will tell you "try management — you can always go back to IC if you don't like it." This is technically true but practically harder than it sounds. After 2-3 years in management:

- Your technical skills will have atrophied (not disappeared, but dulled)
- Hiring managers may view you as a "former IC" rather than a current one
- You may need to take a level cut going back (Staff Engineer to Senior, for example)
- You have built management skills and relationships that feel wasteful to abandon

None of this means you should not try management. It means you should try it with open eyes, not as a "why not?" experiment.

### Role Comparison

| Dimension | Engineering Manager | Tech Lead | Staff Engineer |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|----------------|
| **Leverage** | Through people and process | Through technical decisions and mentoring | Through technical vision and execution |
| **Day-to-day** | 1:1s, planning, hiring, reviews, meetings | Code reviews, design, mentoring, some coding | Design docs, prototypes, cross-team alignment, coding |
| **What you own** | Team health, delivery, people development | Team's technical quality and direction | Cross-team technical outcomes |
| **Hardest part** | Difficult conversations, letting go of coding | Balancing technical work with leadership | Influencing without direct authority |
| **Measured by** | Team output, retention, hiring, delivery | Team technical excellence, system quality | Cross-team technical impact, multiplier effect |

### The Pendulum Career

Some of the most effective senior leaders in engineering have alternated between IC and management roles throughout their careers. This "pendulum" approach has real advantages:

- **As a manager who has been a recent IC:** You have credibility with engineers, you understand what they need, and you can evaluate technical proposals yourself.
- **As an IC who has been a recent manager:** You understand organizational dynamics, you know how decisions get made, and you can navigate politics effectively.

If you are uncertain, consider trying management for 2 years with the explicit plan to evaluate afterward. Discuss this plan with your manager upfront so the transition back (if needed) is not awkward.

## 4. Building Your Promotion Case

The single most important thing to understand about promotions: **they are evidence-based**. A promotion is not a reward for time served or for being a good person. It is a recognition that you are already consistently operating at the next level.

### The Operating-at-the-Next-Level Principle

Promotion committees (formal or informal) ask one question: "Is this person already doing the job of the next level?" If the answer is yes and there is strong evidence, the promotion happens. If the answer is "they could do it" or "they deserve it," it does not.

This means your promotion work starts 6-12 months before you want to be promoted, not when you submit the packet.

### The Promotion Packet

Whether your company has a formal promotion process or not, build a promotion packet. If the process is formal, you need one. If it is informal, having one makes your manager's job easy and dramatically increases your chances.

**Structure:**

```
PROMOTION PACKET

Name: [Your name]
Current level: [e.g., Senior Engineer / L4]
Target level: [e.g., Staff Engineer / L5]
Date: [Date]
Manager: [Manager name]

---

1. WHAT THE TARGET LEVEL MEANS

[Copy your company's level expectations for the target level. If your company
does not have written expectations, write your own understanding and validate
it with your manager.]

Key dimensions at [target level]:
- Scope: [what scope is expected]
- Technical leadership: [what is expected]
- Influence: [what is expected]
- Execution: [what is expected]

---

2. EVIDENCE: KEY PROJECTS

Project 1: [Project Name]
- What I did: [Your specific role and contributions — be precise]
- Scope: [Why this was [target level] scope, not [current level] scope]
- Impact: [Quantified where possible: revenue, latency, reliability,
  developer productivity, etc.]
- Who benefited: [Which teams, users, or business outcomes]
- What was hard: [Technical or organizational challenges you navigated]
- Collaborators who can speak to this: [Names]

Project 2: [Project Name]
[Same structure]

Project 3: [Project Name]
[Same structure]

[Include 3-5 projects. Quality over quantity.]

---

3. ONGOING IMPACT

- Mentoring: [Who you have mentored and their growth]
- Technical leadership: [Design reviews, architecture decisions,
  standards you have set]
- Cross-team work: [Collaborations, alignments, shared infrastructure]
- Culture contributions: [Hiring, onboarding, documentation,
  process improvements]

---

4. PEER AND STAKEHOLDER FEEDBACK

[Include 3-5 quotes from people you have worked with. Ask them directly:
"I am putting together my promotion case. Could you share specific feedback
about working with me on [project]?"]

- [Name, role]: "[Quote]"
- [Name, role]: "[Quote]"

---

5. GROWTH AREAS

[Show self-awareness. Nobody expects perfection. Identify 1-2 areas
where you are still growing into the target level.]

- [Area]: [What you are doing to improve]

---

6. SUMMARY

[2-3 sentences: why you believe you are operating at [target level]
and what you will focus on at that level going forward.]
```

### Common Promotion Blockers

**"You're great at X but we need to see Y."** This is the most common blocker. You might be the best coder on the team but have not demonstrated cross-team influence. Or you are great at design but have not shown you can execute under pressure. Identify the gap early — ask your manager directly: "What is the one thing that would make my promotion case airtight?"

**"There's no opportunity at that level."** Sometimes your current team does not have problems that require Staff-level work. Options: create the opportunity by identifying a cross-team problem that needs solving, or transfer to a team that has Staff-level problems. Do not wait years hoping the opportunity appears.

**"The project didn't ship."** Canceled projects are not wasted promotion evidence. Document what you delivered before cancellation, the technical decisions you made, the learnings, and the partial value. A well-run project that got canceled due to strategy changes still demonstrates your capabilities.

**"I don't have metrics."** Not every impact is quantifiable. "Led the migration of 3 teams from monolith to microservices, establishing patterns now used by 12 teams" is compelling even without a latency number. Combine quantitative and qualitative evidence.

### Working With Your Manager

Your manager is your primary ally in the promotion process (or should be). Have explicit promotion conversations:

- **Quarterly:** "Am I on track for promotion to [level]? What gaps do you see?"
- **After major projects:** "How would this project land in a promotion discussion?"
- **When feedback comes:** "How does this feedback relate to [target level] expectations?"

If your manager is not supportive of your promotion and you believe you have the evidence, consider whether you have the right manager — not all managers are good at (or interested in) advocating for their reports.

### Self-Nomination

In many companies, especially larger ones, **you** must drive your own promotion. Your manager may be supportive but will not do the work for you. This means:

- You write the promotion packet (your manager reviews it)
- You collect peer feedback (your manager supplements it)
- You identify the evidence (your manager validates it)
- You set the timeline (your manager confirms feasibility)

Do not assume your manager is tracking your accomplishments with the same fidelity you are. They have 5-10 other reports and their own career to manage.

## 5. The Brag Document

The brag document (a term popularized by Julia Evans) is the single most valuable career tool most engineers are not using. It is a running record of everything you accomplish, updated regularly.

### Why You Need One

You will forget 80% of what you did by the time performance reviews come around. The human brain is not built to remember six months of incremental work. Without a brag document, your self-review will disproportionately reflect the last 2-3 weeks, and you will undersell yourself.

### Template

```markdown
# Brag Document — [Your Name]

Last updated: [Date]

## Current Goals
- [Goal 1 for this cycle/quarter]
- [Goal 2]
- [Goal 3]

---

## [Year] — [Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4] (or use months)

### Projects Delivered

**[Project Name]** — [One-line summary]
- What I did: [Specifics]
- Impact: [Metrics or qualitative outcomes]
- Scope: [Team / cross-team / org-wide]
- Challenges: [What was hard about this]
- Date: [When it shipped]

### Technical Decisions & Designs

- [Date]: Wrote design doc for [X], adopted by [teams].
  Key decision: [what you chose and why]
- [Date]: Led architecture review for [X].
  Outcome: [what changed as a result]

### Mentoring & Growing Others

- Mentoring [Name]: [What you focused on, what progress they made]
- Onboarded [Name]: [How you helped them ramp up]
- Ran [workshop/talk] on [topic] for [audience]

### Process & Culture Improvements

- Created [tool/process/document] that [impact]
- Improved [workflow] resulting in [outcome]

### Cross-Team Collaboration

- Worked with [Team X] on [what], resulting in [outcome]
- Represented our team in [initiative/working group]

### Writing & Communication

- Published [blog post/RFC/ADR] on [topic]
- Presented [talk] at [venue]
- Wrote [documentation] used by [who]

### Incident Response

- Responded to [incident]: [your role, outcome, follow-up]

### Feedback Received

- "[Quote]" — [Name, context]
- "[Quote]" — [Name, context]

### Miscellaneous

- [Anything else that does not fit above but you want to remember]

---

## [Previous Quarter]
[Same structure]
```

### How to Maintain It

**Option A: Weekly ritual (recommended).** Every Friday, spend 10 minutes adding to your brag document. What did you do this week? What decisions did you make? Who did you help?

**Option B: Bi-weekly.** Every other Friday, spend 15-20 minutes. You will forget more with this cadence, but it is still vastly better than nothing.

**Option C: Event-driven.** Add entries immediately after significant events: shipping a project, writing a design doc, getting positive feedback, handling an incident. Set a calendar reminder to review and fill gaps monthly.

### When to Use It

- **Performance reviews:** Your brag document is the raw material for your self-review. You will write a better review in half the time.
- **Promotion packets:** Pull your strongest evidence directly from the brag document.
- **1:1s with your manager:** Reference specific accomplishments when discussing your trajectory.
- **Resume updates:** When it is time to update your resume, you have a detailed record to draw from instead of trying to remember what you did two years ago.
- **Job interviews:** Behavioral interview questions ("Tell me about a time when...") are trivially easy when you have a brag document full of concrete examples.

## 6. Influence Without Authority

Staff+ engineers, Tech Leads without management authority, and anyone who wants to drive change across an organization needs to master influence without authority. You cannot mandate adoption of your ideas — you have to earn it.

### How to Build Influence

**Write the document nobody else wrote.** When there is a problem everyone complains about but nobody addresses, write the RFC, the design doc, or the ADR. The person who frames the problem controls the conversation. Your proposal does not have to be perfect — it has to be specific enough that people can react to it.

**Build the prototype that proves the idea works.** A working prototype is worth a thousand slides. If you believe the team should adopt a new testing framework, do not write a proposal — build a proof of concept that shows it working in your codebase with real tests. Make it easy for others to try.

**Be the person who fixes things.** Credibility comes from demonstrated competence. Fix the flaky test suite. Clean up the deployment pipeline. Resolve the tech debt that has been annoying everyone. These actions build a reputation that makes people trust your judgment on bigger decisions.

**Build relationships across teams.** Know who the key engineers are on adjacent teams. Understand what they are working on and what their pain points are. When you need buy-in for a cross-team initiative, it helps enormously to already have relationships with the people you need to influence.

**Teach and mentor.** Teaching is the most scalable form of influence. When you run a workshop, write a guide, or mentor someone, you multiply your impact. People who learn from you naturally adopt your approaches and advocate for your ideas.

**Write documentation.** The person who writes the docs shapes how people think about a system. If you want to influence how a service is used, write the definitive guide to it. If you want to change how the team approaches testing, write the testing philosophy doc.

### The "Earn, Don't Claim" Principle

Influence follows demonstrated competence, not declared authority. Saying "I am the technical lead on this" means nothing if people do not trust your judgment. Instead:

- Ship things that work
- Give feedback that makes code better
- Propose solutions that account for constraints others raise
- Follow through on commitments
- Credit others publicly

Over time, people will naturally defer to your opinion on topics where you have earned credibility. This is more durable than any title.

### Navigating Disagreements

**Disagree and commit.** When a decision goes against your recommendation, commit fully to making it work. Do not sabotage it or say "I told you so" when problems arise. Your willingness to disagree openly and then commit fully builds enormous trust.

**Know when to escalate.** Most disagreements should be resolved between the people involved. Escalate only when: the stakes are genuinely high, you have tried to resolve it directly, and the disagreement reflects a fundamental difference in direction (not just preference).

**Pick your battles.** You have a finite amount of political capital. Spend it on things that genuinely matter. If you fight every decision, people stop listening. If you only push back on things that truly matter, people take your objections seriously.

### Building Allies

Find people across the organization who share your goals. If you care about testing quality, find the other engineers who do too. If you want to improve the developer experience, find allies on the platform team. Aligned interests create natural coalitions that are more effective than any individual advocate.

## 7. Navigating Reorgs and Layoffs

Organizational change is not a bug — it is a feature of working at any company that is growing, shrinking, or adapting to market conditions. Expect a significant reorganization every 12-18 months at large companies, and prepare accordingly.

### How to Survive a Reorg

**Have a clear narrative about your value.** When reorgs happen, people make quick decisions about where individuals fit. If you can articulate "I am the person who [specific value you provide]" in one sentence, you are more likely to land in a good position.

**Build relationships outside your immediate team.** If your entire network is on your current team, you are vulnerable when that team is restructured. Build genuine relationships with engineers, managers, and leaders across the organization. These relationships are your safety net.

**Stay visible.** People who are known across the organization have more options during a reorg. This does not mean self-promotion — it means doing impactful work and making sure the right people know about it through design docs, presentations, and cross-team collaboration.

**Be adaptable.** The engineers who navigate reorgs best are the ones who can say "this is not what I planned, but I can make it work" genuinely. Flexibility is a survival trait in large organizations.

### Layoff Preparation (Do This Now, Not When Rumors Start)

**Always have an updated resume.** Update it every quarter when you update your brag document. You should be able to share your resume within 24 hours of any event.

**Maintain your network.** Stay in touch with former colleagues, attend industry events (even occasionally), and be active in professional communities. Your next job is more likely to come from someone you know than from a job board.

**Keep your skills current.** If your day job uses a legacy stack, spend some time on weekends or side projects staying current with modern tools and practices. You do not want to be out of date when you need to interview.

**Save money.** The standard advice of 3-6 months of expenses in savings is especially important in tech, where job searches can take 2-4 months. If you can, target 6 months.

### Signs a Reorg or Layoff Is Coming

No single signal is definitive, but multiple signals together should put you on alert:

- **Hiring freeze:** The company stops all hiring or dramatically slows it
- **Sudden management changes:** Your skip-level or their peers leave or are reassigned
- **"Alignment" meetings:** Leadership schedules mysterious all-hands or strategy sessions with vague agendas
- **Cost-cutting focus:** Sudden attention to cloud costs, tool licenses, and headcount efficiency
- **Leadership departures:** Senior leaders leaving "to pursue other opportunities" in quick succession
- **External signals:** Missed earnings, stock price decline, negative press about the company's business

### If You Are Laid Off

**Negotiate severance.** Even if the company offers a standard package, you can sometimes negotiate more — especially if you have institutional knowledge, if the layoff timing is bad, or if you are willing to help with a transition period.

**File for unemployment immediately.** There is no shame in this. You paid into the system through your taxes. File on day one.

**Update LinkedIn the same day.** Change your status to "Open to Work" (you can make this visible only to recruiters if you prefer). Post a brief message — your network wants to help, but they need to know you are looking.

**Reach out to your network within 48 hours.** Send a short, direct message to former colleagues, mentors, and industry contacts: "I was part of a layoff at [Company]. I am looking for [type of role]. If you know of anything or can make an introduction, I would really appreciate it." Most people genuinely want to help.

**Take a few days before interviewing.** Process the emotional impact before jumping into interviews. A week of rest and reflection will make you a better candidate than panic-applying the day after.

## 8. Switching Jobs Strategically

Job changes are the most powerful lever you have for career growth — in terms of scope, compensation, learning, and trajectory. But switching for the wrong reasons or at the wrong time can set you back.

### When to Leave

- **Learning has plateaued.** You are no longer growing technically or professionally, and there is no path to change that on your current team.
- **No growth path.** The next level up is blocked (no headcount, no scope, no supportive manager) and lateral moves within the company do not solve it.
- **Values misalignment.** The company's direction, culture, or ethics conflict with your own in ways that are not going to change.
- **Compensation significantly below market.** If you are 20%+ below market and internal adjustments cannot close the gap, the market is telling you something.
- **Toxic culture.** Sustained toxicity — not a bad quarter, but a pattern of poor management, burnout culture, or hostile environment — is a valid reason to leave immediately.

### When NOT to Leave

- **You are bored.** Try switching teams or projects first. Boredom often means you need a new challenge, not a new company.
- **You had one bad quarter.** Every job has rough patches. One bad project, one difficult manager interaction, or one missed promotion is not a pattern.
- **You have not been there a year.** Short stints raise questions in interviews. The exception: truly toxic or unethical environments, or layoff-driven departures.
- **You are chasing a title.** A title at a less-known company is worth less than a track record at a strong one. Optimize for learning and impact, not title inflation.

### Job Search Timeline

Expect the full process to take 2-4 months:

- **Weeks 1-2:** Update resume, identify target companies, start reaching out to network
- **Weeks 3-6:** Apply, do recruiter screens, start interview preparation in parallel
- **Weeks 6-10:** On-site interviews (typically 2-4 companies in parallel)
- **Weeks 10-14:** Offers, negotiation, decision, and notice period

### Resume for Engineers

Your resume should focus on **impact**, not responsibilities.

**Bad:** "Worked on performance improvements for the search service."

**Good:** "Reduced p99 search latency from 2s to 200ms by implementing a multi-tier caching layer, improving conversion rate by 3% ($2M annual revenue impact)."

**Bad:** "Responsible for the payments microservice."

**Good:** "Designed and built the payments service handling $50M/month in transactions with 99.99% uptime, reducing payment failures by 40%."

Structure each bullet as: **[Action verb] [what you did] [quantified impact]**.

### Evaluating Offers

Rank these factors by your current priorities (they change over time):

1. **Total compensation:** Base salary + equity + annual bonus + signing bonus. Do not compare base salary alone — equity can be 30-60% of total comp at senior levels.
2. **Team and manager:** A great manager at an average company beats an average manager at a great company. Ask to meet the team before accepting.
3. **Growth opportunity:** Will this role stretch you? Will you learn new things? Is there a path to the next level?
4. **Work-life balance:** What are the actual working hours? What is the on-call burden? What is the PTO culture (not policy — culture)?
5. **Mission alignment:** Do you care about what the company does? This matters more than people think, especially over multi-year tenures.

### Counter-Offers

The conventional wisdom is to not accept counter-offers, and it is usually right. The reasons you wanted to leave — the manager, the culture, the lack of growth — will still be there after the raise. Counter-offers also flag you as a flight risk, which can affect future opportunities internally.

The exception: if your only reason for leaving was compensation and you genuinely love everything else about the role, a counter-offer can make sense. But be honest with yourself about whether money was really the only factor.

## 9. Negotiation

Negotiation is a skill, not a personality trait. You can learn it, practice it, and get better at it. The single most important rule: **always negotiate**. The worst outcome is they say "this is our best offer," and you are no worse off than before you asked.

### Why You Must Negotiate

- Companies expect you to negotiate. The initial offer is almost never the best they can do.
- A $10K difference in base salary, compounded over a career, is worth hundreds of thousands of dollars.
- Negotiating signals confidence and self-awareness — qualities companies want in senior engineers.
- Offers are rarely rescinded for negotiating professionally. If a company rescinds an offer because you asked reasonable questions, that tells you something about the culture.

### Research Market Rates

Before any negotiation, know the market. Use multiple sources:

- **levels.fyi:** The best source for tech compensation data by company and level
- **Glassdoor:** Useful for non-tech-giant companies
- **Blind:** Anonymous professional network with compensation threads (filter for noise)
- **Pragmatic Engineer's compensation data:** Curated and reliable
- **Your network:** Ask trusted peers (this gets easier with seniority)

### What to Negotiate

Think about total compensation, not just base salary:

| Component | Flexibility | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Base salary | Medium | Often has band limits per level |
| Equity/RSUs | High | Especially at pre-IPO companies |
| Signing bonus | High | Easiest component to increase |
| Annual bonus | Low | Usually formulaic |
| Relocation | Medium-High | If applicable |
| Title/Level | Medium | Can significantly affect future comp |
| PTO | Low-Medium | Sometimes negotiable at senior levels |
| Remote flexibility | Medium | Increasingly negotiable |
| Start date | High | Easy win if you need time between jobs |

### Techniques

**Have a competing offer.** This is the single strongest negotiating lever. If you can say "I have an offer from [Company] at [amount]," you shift the dynamic from "what will you accept?" to "what will it take to win you?"

**Name a range, not a number.** "Based on my research and experience, I am targeting $X to $Y for total compensation." The bottom of your range should be what you actually want. They will anchor to the bottom, so set it accordingly.

**Ask for specific things.** Instead of "can you do better?", try: "Is there flexibility on the equity component?" or "Could we explore a higher signing bonus to bridge the gap?" Specific asks get specific answers.

**If they cannot move on base, ask about other components.** "I understand the base salary is at band maximum. Could we look at an additional equity grant, a signing bonus, or an accelerated review cycle?"

**Use silence.** After making your ask, stop talking. Silence is uncomfortable, and people often fill it by making concessions. Do not negotiate against yourself.

### What NOT to Do

**Do not share your current salary.** It anchors the conversation against you. In many jurisdictions, it is illegal for employers to ask. If pressed, redirect: "I would prefer to focus on the value I will bring to this role and what the market rate is."

**Do not accept on the spot.** Always ask for time: "Thank you for this offer. I am very excited about the opportunity. Could I have a few days to review the details?" This gives you time to negotiate thoughtfully.

**Do not lie about competing offers.** If you claim to have an offer you do not have, and they call your bluff (or ask for proof, which some companies do), you lose all credibility.

**Do not be adversarial.** Negotiation is a collaboration to find a package that works for both sides. Frame it that way: "I am very excited about this role. I want to make sure the compensation reflects the value I will bring so I can fully commit."

### Get Everything in Writing

Before accepting any offer, get the complete package in writing: base salary, equity (number of shares, vesting schedule, strike price if options), signing bonus, annual bonus target, start date, title, and any special agreements (remote work, review timeline, etc.). Verbal promises are forgotten. Written offers are binding.

## 10. Building Your Brand

Your professional brand is not about self-promotion. It is about being known for something specific by the people who matter in your field. The best opportunities in your career will come from people who know your work, not from job boards.

### Write About What You Are Learning

You do not need to be an expert to write. Some of the most valuable technical blog posts are written by people who just figured something out and are explaining it while it is fresh. Write about:

- Problems you solved and how you solved them
- Comparisons of tools or approaches you evaluated
- Lessons learned from failures or incidents (with appropriate discretion)
- Explanations of concepts you found confusing until you understood them

Publish on your own blog, on your company's engineering blog (great for visibility), or on platforms like dev.to or Medium. Consistency matters more than volume — one good post per month beats a burst of five posts followed by six months of silence.

### Speak at Meetups and Conferences

Start small and build up:

1. **Lightning talks at your company:** 5-minute presentations to your team or engineering org
2. **Local meetups:** 20-30 minute talks at community events
3. **Regional conferences:** 30-45 minute talks at mid-size events
4. **Major conferences:** Once you have a track record and a compelling topic

The barrier to entry for meetup talks is low. Most meetup organizers are desperate for speakers. Volunteer, and you will likely get a slot.

### Build a Portfolio

Quality over quantity. Two or three well-documented, well-crafted projects are worth more than 50 abandoned repositories. For each portfolio project:

- Write a clear README explaining what it does and why
- Include a demo (deployed app, screenshots, or video)
- Show clean, well-tested code
- Explain your technical decisions

### Help Others Publicly

Answer questions on Stack Overflow. Help people in GitHub issues. Participate in relevant Discord or Slack communities. Comment thoughtfully on technical posts. This builds your reputation as someone who is knowledgeable and generous with their time — a combination that attracts great opportunities.

### Contribute to Open Source

Open source contributions are one of the most visible forms of professional credibility. See Chapter 28 for a detailed guide. Even small contributions — documentation fixes, bug reports, test improvements — make you visible to maintainers and other contributors.

### The Long Game

Building a professional brand is a multi-year endeavor. The compounding works like this:

1. **Year 1:** You write a few posts, give a talk or two, contribute to a project. A small number of people in your local community know your name.
2. **Year 2-3:** You are a regular at meetups, you have a handful of well-received posts, and people start reaching out to you for advice or collaboration.
3. **Year 4-5:** You are a known quantity in your niche. Recruiters reach out with genuinely interesting opportunities (not spam). Conference organizers invite you to speak. People reference your work in conversations.
4. **Year 5+:** Opportunities find you. You can choose your next role rather than compete for it. Your reputation precedes you in interviews.

The best time to start building your brand was five years ago. The second best time is today. Start with one action this week: write a post, submit a talk proposal, or contribute to an open source project.

### Your Brand Is Your Reputation

Ultimately, your brand is what people say about you when you are not in the room. It is built through:

- **Consistency:** Showing up regularly, delivering reliably, following through on commitments
- **Quality:** Doing work you are proud of, holding yourself to high standards
- **Generosity:** Helping others without expecting immediate returns
- **Authenticity:** Being honest about what you know and what you do not

You cannot shortcut this with personal branding tricks. There is no substitute for doing great work and being a good colleague over an extended period. The "brand building" activities — writing, speaking, contributing — are amplifiers for the reputation you have already earned through your work.

## Putting It All Together

Career engineering is not about optimizing for promotions or maximizing compensation (though those are fine goals). It is about being intentional with the finite time you have. Here is a minimal career maintenance routine:

**Weekly (10 minutes):**
- Update your brag document

**Quarterly (1 hour):**
- Review your brag document and update your resume
- Have a career conversation with your manager
- Assess: am I learning? Am I growing? Am I valued?

**Annually (half a day):**
- Evaluate your trajectory: is this the right company, team, and role?
- Update your promotion packet (if applicable)
- Set career goals for the next year
- Review your compensation against market data

**Ongoing:**
- Build relationships across your organization and industry
- Write, teach, and share what you learn
- Help others — it comes back around

The engineers who have the best careers are not the ones who are the best at coding (though they are usually very good). They are the ones who are intentional about where they are going, build evidence of their impact, and invest in relationships. You can do all of this without being political, without being self-promotional, and without compromising your technical depth. It just takes the same discipline you apply to your code.
