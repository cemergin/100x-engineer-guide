# Chapter 29: Career Engineering

> **Part II — Applied Engineering** | Prerequisites: None | Difficulty: All levels

Here is something nobody tells you when you are starting out: your career is a system. It has inputs, feedback loops, latency, and failure modes — just like the software you build. The engineers who have the best careers are not the luckiest or the most naturally talented. They are the ones who treat their career with the same rigor they apply to their code. They instrument it, they iterate on it, and they debug it when it goes wrong.

I have watched brilliant engineers get passed over for promotions they deserved because they could not articulate their impact. I have watched mediocre engineers accelerate past them because they understood how the game worked. The goal of this chapter is to make sure you never fall into the first category.

You do not have to be political. You do not have to be a self-promoter. You do not have to compromise your technical depth. But you do have to be intentional. Career outcomes are not random — they are a function of your inputs. This chapter is about designing those inputs deliberately.

## 1. The Engineering Career Ladder

Every engineering organization has a career ladder, whether it is formally documented or not. Understanding the ladder is the first step to navigating it deliberately rather than hoping for the best.

Think of the career ladder as a specification document. Just like a well-written spec tells you what "done" looks like, the ladder tells you what "operating at this level" means. Most engineers never read the spec. They just write code and hope someone notices. Do not be that engineer.

### Typical Levels

| Level | Title (varies by company) | Typical Experience | Core Expectation |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| L1/L2 | Junior / Associate Engineer | 0-2 years | Complete well-defined tasks with guidance |
| L3 | Mid-level Engineer | 2-5 years | Own features, design within boundaries |
| L4 | Senior Engineer | 5-10 years | Own systems, influence team direction, mentor |
| L5 | Staff Engineer | 8-15 years | Own cross-team technical direction, define problems |
| L6 | Principal Engineer | 12-20+ years | Org-wide technical strategy, anticipate problems years ahead |
| L7 | Distinguished / Fellow | 15-25+ years | Company-wide or industry-wide impact |

> **Note:** The numbers above are rough guides. Some people reach Staff in 6 years; others are happy staying Senior for 20. Neither path is wrong.

### What Changes at Each Level

Here is the key insight that most engineers miss: **the career ladder is not about doing the same things better — it is about doing fundamentally different things**. A Senior who is really good at feature work does not automatically become a Staff. Staff is a different job, not a more impressive version of Senior.

**Junior (L1/L2):** You are given well-scoped tasks. Your job is to learn the codebase, the tools, and how to ship reliable code. Success means completing tickets on time, asking good questions, and writing code that does not create extra work for others. The trap here is thinking that going fast is what matters. What actually matters is building the habits — code review etiquette, asking for help at the right time, documenting your work — that will serve you for the rest of your career.

**Mid-level (L3):** You own entire features from design to delivery. You can take a product requirement, break it into technical tasks, and build it. You design within the boundaries of existing architecture. You start reviewing others' code and contributing to team discussions. The trap here is staying in execution mode. Mid-level engineers who want to get to Senior need to start *thinking* about the team, not just their own work. Are there patterns causing bugs across the codebase? Is there a testing gap? Start caring about those things.

**Senior (L4):** You own systems, not just features. You make technical decisions that affect the whole team. You mentor junior and mid-level engineers. You are trusted to handle ambiguity — someone can give you a vague problem and you will figure out the right approach. You influence team direction through proposals, design docs, and technical leadership. The trap at this level is doing everything yourself because it is faster. Leverage matters more than raw output at this level. (See Ch 9 on engineering leadership for how this plays out in practice.)

**Staff (L5):** You own cross-team technical direction. The critical shift here is that you **define problems**, not just solve them. You see a gap nobody else has articulated, write the proposal, build consensus, and drive it to completion. Your impact goes beyond your immediate team. The trap at this level is writing great proposals that never get implemented. Influence without authority is the core skill. More on this in Section 6.

**Principal (L6):** You set org-wide technical strategy. You anticipate problems 1-3 years out and steer the organization to avoid them. You shape how hundreds of engineers work. Your influence is often exercised through architecture reviews, technical strategy documents, and mentoring Staff engineers. The trap here is operating at Staff level — working on problems that are important today rather than the ones that will matter in two years.

**Distinguished / Fellow (L7):** You are recognized as a leading expert in your domain, often across the industry. You represent the company externally. You work on problems that define the company's technical future. Very few roles exist at this level. If you are reading this chapter, you are probably not yet at this level and the path there is less about deliberate career planning and more about exceptional technical contributions over a long career.

### The Scope Dimension

The simplest way to understand the career ladder: **your impact radius grows**.

```
Junior:     Your code
Mid:        Your feature
Senior:     Your team's systems
Staff:      Multiple teams' technical direction
Principal:  Org-wide technical strategy
Fellow:     Company-wide or industry-wide influence
```

Every promotion is fundamentally about demonstrating you can operate at a wider scope — and operating at a wider scope requires fundamentally different skills. The engineer who is exceptional at writing clean, performant code within a bounded problem is not automatically the engineer who can navigate the organizational dynamics required to get three teams to agree on a shared API standard. Both skills are valuable. They are not the same skill.

Here is another lens: the *cost of a mistake* also grows. A Junior's bad commit breaks a feature. A Senior's bad architectural decision costs three months of engineering time. A Principal's wrong technical bet can cost the company a year and millions of dollars. The career ladder is calibrated to trust level as much as skill level. You earn wider trust by demonstrating judgment — technical and organizational — at progressively larger stakes.

### Reading Your Company's Ladder Critically

Most companies have a career ladder document. Most engineers never read it carefully. This is a mistake.

Read your company's ladder with these questions:

**What are the distinguishing characteristics?** What specifically separates L4 from L5? Not "more senior" or "broader impact" — what concrete behaviors or outcomes define the boundary?

**What verbs does it use?** "Leads" vs "contributes to." "Defines" vs "executes." "Influences" vs "follows." The verbs are the signal. If you are still contributing and executing at a level where the expectation is to lead and define, you have your gap.

**Is there explicit cross-team scope at the target level?** For most companies, the jump to Staff (L5) is explicitly when scope must go beyond your immediate team. If you are not working across teams, you are not building Staff evidence no matter how technically excellent you are.

**What does "influence" mean at each level?** Influence is often described vaguely in level expectations. Push your manager to be specific: "When the ladder says 'influences technical direction,' what does that look like at this company? What have recent Staff-level promotions done that demonstrated that?"

If your company does not have a written ladder (this is more common at smaller companies), write your own understanding of the level expectations and validate it explicitly with your manager. A shared mental model of what "next level" means is a prerequisite for building a convincing promotion case.

### Time Between Levels

Typical timelines (these vary significantly by company and individual):

- **Junior to Mid:** 1-2 years
- **Mid to Senior:** 2-4 years
- **Senior to Staff:** 3-6 years (often longer)
- **Staff to Principal:** 4-8 years (if ever — very few roles)

A word on these numbers: they represent time at the level with consistent strong performance, not just time in the industry. I have seen engineers who spent 8 years at "Senior" without doing Senior-level work — they were doing good Junior work, just longer. Time in the seat is not evidence of anything by itself. Impact at the target level's scope is what moves you forward.

### Senior Is a Terminal Level (and That Is Fine)

At most companies, Senior Engineer is the **terminal level** — meaning you can stay there indefinitely without any expectation to promote. This is not a consolation prize. Senior is the level where you have deep technical autonomy, strong compensation, and the freedom to focus on craft. Many of the best engineers you will ever work with are long-tenured Seniors who chose depth over breadth.

The question is not "how do I get to Staff?" — it is "what do I want my day-to-day work to look like?" If the answer is "I want to write great code, own systems, and mentor a few people," Senior is an excellent place to be.

There is a corrosive cultural assumption in some engineering orgs that if you are not pushing to the next level, you are not ambitious. That is backwards. Knowing what you want and optimizing for it is exactly as ambitious as chasing a title you do not actually want.

## 2. Staff+ Engineer Archetypes

Will Larson's framework (from *Staff Engineer: Leadership beyond the management track*) identifies four archetypes of Staff+ engineers. Most Staff+ roles are a blend of these, but understanding the archetypes helps you figure out where you fit and — more importantly — how to signal it to your organization.

Here is why this matters: you can be doing legitimate Staff-level work and still not be getting credit for it, because the work you are doing does not match the mental model your manager and skip-level have for what a Staff engineer looks like at your company. Knowing the archetypes helps you either align with the expectation or have an explicit conversation about it.

### The Tech Lead

**What they do:** Drive the technical direction of a specific team. They are close to the code — often writing or reviewing significant portions of it. They balance technical decisions with product and people considerations. They set technical standards for the team and ensure the team ships reliably.

**Day-to-day:** Code reviews, design discussions, sprint planning, 1:1s with engineers on the team, writing technical specs, unblocking teammates, and still writing code regularly.

**Best for you if:** You enjoy being deeply embedded in a team, you care about both the technical and human aspects of shipping software, and you want to stay close to the code while having broader influence.

**Common failure mode:** Getting sucked into execution so thoroughly that you stop doing the leadership work. The best Tech Leads hold the technical vision of the team in their head even when they are down in the code. The worst ones become very senior engineers who are technically excellent but not actually leading anything.

**The story:** One of the best Tech Leads I have seen was someone who had turned down two Staff promotions because he liked being in the code. He ran design reviews like clockwork, maintained an architectural decision record that was the envy of the org, and had the lowest bug rate in production of any team at the company. His org made the staff track work for him rather than asking him to become someone he was not. This is the outcome worth optimizing for — not a title, but work that genuinely fits you.

### The Architect

**What they do:** Define cross-team or org-wide technical vision. They design systems that span multiple teams, ensure architectural consistency, and make technology choices that affect the whole organization. They may not write much production code day-to-day.

**Day-to-day:** Architecture reviews, writing and reviewing design documents, meeting with multiple teams to align technical direction, evaluating new technologies, creating reference architectures, and mentoring senior engineers on system design.

**Best for you if:** You think in systems, you enjoy the design phase more than the implementation phase, and you are comfortable influencing through documents and conversations rather than pull requests.

**Common failure mode:** Designing systems that are theoretically elegant but ignore the realities of the teams that have to implement them. Great Architects build relationships with the engineers doing the implementation and design around their constraints, not in spite of them. The "ivory tower architect" failure mode — beautiful diagrams, unmaintainable reality — is extremely common and extremely costly.

**The story:** I watched a brilliant architect spend six months designing a microservices decomposition that was technically correct but required all five product teams to freeze their roadmaps for a quarter to implement it. Zero teams adopted it. The problem was not the design — it was the process. She had not brought the teams in early enough to build buy-in or understand their constraints. The redesign she did six months later, with those teams as co-designers, shipped within the quarter. The technical content was similar. The process was completely different. (See Ch 27 for how to write design documents that actually get read and implemented.)

### The Solver

**What they do:** Parachute into the hardest problems the organization faces. They operate across teams as needed, going wherever the most critical technical challenge is. They unblock others by tackling problems nobody else can solve.

**Day-to-day:** Deep investigation of hard problems (performance, reliability, security), prototyping solutions, writing code in unfamiliar codebases, teaching teams how to maintain the solution after they move on.

**Best for you if:** You thrive on variety, you are energized by hard problems, you are comfortable being a stranger on a new team frequently, and you do not need to see the long-term life of the systems you work on.

**Common failure mode:** Solving problems and moving on without ensuring the team can maintain the solution. A Solver who does not teach is just creating new dependencies. The best Solvers write excellent runbooks, pair extensively with the team they are helping, and make themselves unnecessary before they leave.

**This archetype is rare and highly valued.** The engineers who can walk into any codebase, understand it quickly, and fix the thing nobody else could fix are extraordinarily valuable. If this sounds like you, lean into it — and make sure your manager understands this is your superpower, not a sign that you cannot commit.

### The Right Hand

**What they do:** Extend the capacity of a VP or Director of Engineering. They work on whatever matters most to the org right now — which changes frequently. They represent engineering leadership in rooms where the VP cannot be present.

**Day-to-day:** Highly variable. One week it might be leading a technical due diligence for an acquisition, the next it might be driving an org-wide migration, the next it might be debugging a political problem between two teams.

**Best for you if:** You are comfortable with ambiguity, you enjoy organizational problems as much as technical ones, and you are willing to work on whatever is most impactful rather than what is most interesting to you personally.

**Common failure mode:** Losing your technical grounding. The Right Hand role can drift toward pure organizational work if you let it. The best Right Hands maintain enough technical depth to have real credibility with the engineers they are working alongside.

**Important caveat:** This archetype only works if you trust and respect the VP or Director you are extending. If you do not believe in their direction, you will be miserable executing it and ineffective at representing it. Choose your partner carefully before accepting a role like this.

### Self-Assessment: Which Archetype Fits You?

Ask yourself these questions honestly — not "which sounds most impressive" but "which sounds like my best days at work":

1. **When you have free time at work, what do you gravitate toward?** Code (Tech Lead/Solver), design docs (Architect), or organizational problems (Right Hand)?
2. **How do you feel about working on the same team for years?** Energized (Tech Lead), restless (Solver), indifferent (Architect/Right Hand)?
3. **What is your relationship with ambiguity?** You want to reduce it for your team (Tech Lead), you want to resolve it through design (Architect), you seek it out (Solver/Right Hand)?
4. **How much do you care about organizational dynamics?** Necessary evil (Solver), important context (Tech Lead/Architect), genuinely interesting (Right Hand)?
5. **How do you feel when someone else implements a system you designed?** Satisfied (Architect), slightly frustrated (Tech Lead/Solver), entirely fine (Right Hand)?

There are no right answers. The right answer is the one that matches how you actually work, not how you think you should work.

### How to Signal Which Path You Want

Do not wait for your organization to assign you an archetype. Demonstrate the behaviors of the archetype you want:

- **Tech Lead path:** Take ownership of your team's technical standards. Write the on-call playbook. Drive the design reviews. Be the person who ensures quality and that nothing slips through the cracks.
- **Architect path:** Write cross-team design docs without being asked. Propose standards that span teams. Attend architecture reviews outside your team. Build relationships with Tech Leads on other teams.
- **Solver path:** Volunteer for the hairiest incidents and investigations. Help other teams when they are stuck. Build a reputation for cracking hard problems in unfamiliar territory.
- **Right Hand path:** Offer to help your engineering director with special projects. Volunteer for cross-org initiatives. Show you can represent engineering leadership effectively in meetings where they cannot be.

And then — critically — talk to your manager explicitly about which path you are pursuing. Do not just behave differently and hope they notice. Say "I want to grow toward the Architect archetype. Here is how I am trying to demonstrate that. What gaps do you see?" That conversation is the beginning of the promotion process.

## 3. IC vs Management Decision

At some point in your career, someone will ask if you have thought about management. Maybe your manager floats it. Maybe a skip-level mentions it. Maybe the company reorgs and suddenly there is a manager opening and someone is looking at you.

This is one of the most consequential decisions you will make, and it deserves careful thought rather than a reflexive answer in either direction. "Management sounds hard, no thanks" is not analysis. "Sure, I'm ready for the next step" is not analysis either.

Let me be direct: engineering management is a completely different job. It is not a promotion in any meaningful sense. You are trading one set of skills (technical) for another set of skills (people, process, organizational). Most of what made you good as an IC will not help you as a manager. Some of it will actively hurt you.

### Signals That Management Might Suit You

These are not "good at management" signals — they are "might enjoy and be energized by management" signals. There is a difference.

- You find yourself more energized by helping someone else solve a problem than solving it yourself
- You are frustrated by organizational problems (unclear priorities, poor communication, misaligned teams) and want to fix them from the inside
- You enjoy 1:1 conversations about people's growth and goals — genuinely, not as a professional obligation
- You want to set the direction for a team, not just execute on it
- You are comfortable making decisions with incomplete information and owning the outcome
- You have already been informally leading — organizing the team, resolving conflicts, setting priorities — and it felt natural, not like extra work

### Signals to Stay IC

Again, these are not deficiencies. They are honest self-assessments.

- You lose energy in meetings and gain it when coding. Management is mostly meetings.
- You find performance reviews and career conversations draining rather than energizing. Management is a lot of those.
- You want to go deep on technical problems, not wide across organizational ones
- You have a strong intrinsic drive to build things with your own hands
- You measure your best days by the quality of the code or system you produced, not by the conversations you had
- You are interested in management mostly because it seems like "the next step." It is not — Staff Engineer exists for a reason.

The last point is worth dwelling on. A lot of engineers consider management because they do not know about Staff, or because their company's IC track above Senior is thin or unclear. Before going to management, invest 30 minutes understanding whether your company has a genuine Staff+ IC track. If it does, and it fits you, that is your path.

### The "You Can Always Switch Back" Myth

People will tell you "try management — you can always go back to IC if you don't like it." This is technically true but practically harder than it sounds. After 2-3 years in management:

- Your technical skills will have atrophied. Not disappeared, but dulled. The codebase has moved on without you. Your coding velocity has dropped. There is a real cost to re-entry.
- Hiring managers may view you as a "former IC" rather than a current one. Whether this is fair or not, it changes how they evaluate you.
- You may need to take a level cut going back. Staff Engineer to Senior, for example. This affects your ego and your compensation.
- You have built management skills and relationships that feel wasteful to abandon. Sunk cost is real, even when you know it should not be.

I have seen this play out both ways. Some engineers try management, realize quickly it is not for them, and make a clean exit back to IC. Others get two years in and feel trapped — the comp as a manager is higher, the relationships are real, but they are miserable doing the work. Neither of these is a great outcome.

None of this means you should not try management. It means you should try it with open eyes, with a clear hypothesis about what you are trying to learn, and with a plan for evaluation — not as a "why not?" experiment.

### Role Comparison

| Dimension | Engineering Manager | Tech Lead | Staff Engineer |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|----------------|
| **Leverage** | Through people and process | Through technical decisions and mentoring | Through technical vision and execution |
| **Day-to-day** | 1:1s, planning, hiring, reviews, meetings | Code reviews, design, mentoring, some coding | Design docs, prototypes, cross-team alignment, coding |
| **What you own** | Team health, delivery, people development | Team's technical quality and direction | Cross-team technical outcomes |
| **Hardest part** | Difficult conversations, letting go of coding | Balancing technical work with leadership | Influencing without direct authority |
| **Measured by** | Team output, retention, hiring, delivery | Team technical excellence, system quality | Cross-team technical impact, multiplier effect |

### The Pendulum Career

Some of the most effective senior leaders in engineering have alternated between IC and management roles throughout their careers. This "pendulum" approach has real advantages:

- **As a manager who has been a recent IC:** You have credibility with engineers, you understand what they need, and you can evaluate technical proposals yourself. You remember what it feels like to have your code reviewed by someone who does not understand your constraints.
- **As an IC who has been a recent manager:** You understand organizational dynamics, you know how decisions get made, and you can navigate politics effectively. You know what your manager is dealing with and why certain things take longer than they should.

If you are uncertain, consider trying management for 2 years with the explicit plan to evaluate afterward. Discuss this plan with your manager upfront so the transition back (if needed) is not awkward. "I want to try this for two years and see if it is for me" is a completely reasonable thing to say, and any good manager will appreciate the self-awareness.

### Questions to Ask Before Saying Yes to Management

If you are seriously considering a management role, do your diligence. These are not rhetorical — ask them of your potential skip-level and of the engineers who would be on your team:

**To your skip-level:**
- What does success look like for an EM in this role in the first 6 months?
- What happened to the previous manager of this team? (The answer matters a lot.)
- What authority will I have over hiring, compensation, and team composition?
- What is the ratio of "people work" to "technical strategy work" you expect?

**To the engineers on the team:**
- What is going well on the team right now that you would not want a new manager to break?
- What is the biggest thing you wish a manager would fix?
- How often do you feel unblocked vs waiting on dependencies?

**To yourself:**
- Can I name three people's careers I want to actively invest in? (Not in the abstract — specific people.)
- Am I willing to have difficult conversations about performance, even when it is uncomfortable?
- Can I be the person who says "no, we are not building that right now" to a product manager I like and respect?
- What will I do in the first month when an engineer on my team ships something I could have done better myself?

That last one is the real test. Management requires genuine comfort with delegating work you could do yourself to people who will do it differently — and sometimes worse, in the short term — because that is how they grow. If that thought makes you anxious, stay IC a little longer.

### The Mechanics of Switching from Management Back to IC

If you decide management is not for you, here is the practical playbook for switching back:

**Step 1: Have the conversation early.** As soon as you have clarity, talk to your manager. "I have been reflecting on the last [X] months, and I think I want to move back to an IC role. I want to be thoughtful about this transition so it is good for the team and for me." Do not wait until you are miserable.

**Step 2: Identify the right IC role.** Do not just go back to what you were doing before. You have learned things as a manager — organizational dynamics, what makes teams work — that can make you a distinctly better Staff engineer or Tech Lead. Use that.

**Step 3: Be honest in interviews.** "I spent 18 months in engineering management and decided the IC track was a better fit for how I work" is a perfectly legitimate story. It shows self-awareness. Pair it with a clear articulation of what you learned and how it makes you a better IC.

**Step 4: Rebuild your technical credibility actively.** Go deep on something new. Write code. Contribute to open source. Give a technical talk. Signal clearly that you are re-engaged with the craft.

## 4. Building Your Promotion Case

Let me tell you about an engineer I know — I'll call her Maya. By every technical measure, she was operating at Staff level for 18 months. She was the go-to person for the company's most complex distributed systems problems. She had written the design doc that became the foundation of the new event streaming infrastructure. She had unblocked three teams that were stuck. When two of those teams had post-mortems, her name came up as a key contributor.

She did not get promoted to Staff for another 8 months after she clearly deserved it.

The reason? She could not articulate her impact in the language the promotion committee used. She would say things like "I helped with the event streaming work" instead of "I defined and drove the event streaming migration strategy that reduced infrastructure costs by 30% and unblocked the data and payments teams from adopting real-time processing." Same work. Completely different evidence.

Do not be Maya. Not in the second half of that story, anyway — be Maya in the first half: do exceptional work. But also know how to make the case.

### The Operating-at-the-Next-Level Principle

Promotion committees (formal or informal) ask one question: "Is this person already doing the job of the next level?" If the answer is yes and there is strong evidence, the promotion happens. If the answer is "they could do it" or "they deserve it," it does not.

This means your promotion work starts 6-12 months before you want to be promoted, not when you submit the packet. You need to already be operating at that level. The packet is just documentation that you have been.

Here is the corollary that most engineers miss: **operating at the next level does not automatically get you promoted.** You also have to *demonstrate* that you are operating at the next level, in a way that the people making the decision can see and evaluate. This is not political maneuvering — it is basic communication.

### The Promotion Packet

Whether your company has a formal promotion process or not, build a promotion packet. If the process is formal, you need one. If it is informal, having one makes your manager's job easy and dramatically increases your chances.

Think of the promotion packet as a technical proposal — except the thing you are proposing is that you should be promoted. Just like a technical proposal needs to make its case with evidence rather than assertion, your promotion packet needs concrete examples, not adjectives.

**Structure:**

```
PROMOTION PACKET

Name: [Your name]
Current level: [e.g., Senior Engineer / L4]
Target level: [e.g., Staff Engineer / L5]
Date: [Date]
Manager: [Manager name]

---

1. WHAT THE TARGET LEVEL MEANS

[Copy your company's level expectations for the target level. If your company
does not have written expectations, write your own understanding and validate
it with your manager.]

Key dimensions at [target level]:
- Scope: [what scope is expected]
- Technical leadership: [what is expected]
- Influence: [what is expected]
- Execution: [what is expected]

---

2. EVIDENCE: KEY PROJECTS

Project 1: [Project Name]
- What I did: [Your specific role and contributions — be precise]
- Scope: [Why this was [target level] scope, not [current level] scope]
- Impact: [Quantified where possible: revenue, latency, reliability,
  developer productivity, etc.]
- Who benefited: [Which teams, users, or business outcomes]
- What was hard: [Technical or organizational challenges you navigated]
- Collaborators who can speak to this: [Names]

Project 2: [Project Name]
[Same structure]

Project 3: [Project Name]
[Same structure]

[Include 3-5 projects. Quality over quantity.]

---

3. ONGOING IMPACT

- Mentoring: [Who you have mentored and their growth]
- Technical leadership: [Design reviews, architecture decisions,
  standards you have set]
- Cross-team work: [Collaborations, alignments, shared infrastructure]
- Culture contributions: [Hiring, onboarding, documentation,
  process improvements]

---

4. PEER AND STAKEHOLDER FEEDBACK

[Include 3-5 quotes from people you have worked with. Ask them directly:
"I am putting together my promotion case. Could you share specific feedback
about working with me on [project]?"]

- [Name, role]: "[Quote]"
- [Name, role]: "[Quote]"

---

5. GROWTH AREAS

[Show self-awareness. Nobody expects perfection. Identify 1-2 areas
where you are still growing into the target level.]

- [Area]: [What you are doing to improve]

---

6. SUMMARY

[2-3 sentences: why you believe you are operating at [target level]
and what you will focus on at that level going forward.]
```

### The Scope Argument Is the Most Important Argument

In every section of your promotion packet, you need to answer the same question: "Why was this Staff-level work, not Senior-level work?" This is the question the promotion committee is asking, even if implicitly.

Senior engineers write features. Staff engineers define the technical approach that determines which features are possible. Senior engineers fix bugs in their codebase. Staff engineers identify patterns across codebases that prevent classes of bugs. Senior engineers review pull requests for their team. Staff engineers establish code review practices that improve the quality across multiple teams.

The scope argument is not about raw difficulty. A very hard bug fix is still not Staff-level work. What makes something Staff-level is the **scope of impact** — the number of teams, systems, or engineers affected by the decision.

In your packet, be explicit: "This was Staff-level work because it required aligning three teams on a shared approach and the decision I made affected their roadmaps for the next two quarters."

Here is a useful test for each piece of evidence you include:

```
Scope test:
  - How many teams were affected by this work?
  - How many engineers changed their behavior based on what I did?
  - What would have happened if I had not done this?
  - Who else could have done this work? (If many people could, it may not be Senior+ scope)

Impact test:
  - Did this affect a customer-visible metric?
  - Did this unblock work that was blocked?
  - Did this reduce risk or toil at a meaningful scale?
  - Can I quantify any part of this?

Leadership test:
  - Did I define the problem or just solve a problem that was handed to me?
  - Did I drive alignment across people who had different initial opinions?
  - Did I make a decision that required weighing real tradeoffs?
  - Did I leave the system in a better state than I found it, in a way others can build on?
```

Every entry in your promotion packet should pass the scope test, the impact test, or (ideally) both. If it does not, it belongs in the brag document but not in the promotion packet.

### Making the Case Without Metrics

One of the most common anxieties engineers have about their promotion packet is "I do not have metrics for this." The anxiety is understandable but usually overblown. Here is the reality: promotion committees evaluate patterns of evidence, not individual data points. Strong qualitative evidence from multiple sources is more convincing than a single impressive metric.

That said, you should always *try* to find some quantification, even if it is imprecise:

- "Reduced deployment time" → "Reduced deployment time from ~45 minutes to ~8 minutes"
- "Improved reliability" → "Reduced P1 incidents in the payments flow from ~2/month to <1/quarter"
- "Unblocked teams" → "Unblocked 4 teams from adopting the new API — previously blocked for 3 months"
- "Improved developer experience" → "Setup time for new engineers went from 2 days to 2 hours"

Rough is fine. "~45 minutes" is better than "a long time." The point is specificity — it signals that you were paying attention to the actual impact of your work, not just shipping and moving on.

When you genuinely cannot quantify, use structural evidence: adoption, endorsements, and citations. "The testing framework I wrote is now used by 8 teams and has been cited in 12 internal ADRs" is strong qualitative evidence even without a line count or latency number.

### Gathering Peer Feedback That Actually Helps

The peer feedback section of a promotion packet is often the weakest part because engineers ask generic questions and get generic answers. Here is how to get feedback that is specific, usable, and genuinely supportive.

**Ask specific people for specific things.** Instead of "would you write me a recommendation?", try:

> "I am putting together my promotion case. You and I worked closely on the [Project] migration. Could you share specific feedback on [1] how I handled the cross-team alignment, and [2] the technical decisions I drove? Even 2-3 sentences with concrete examples would be incredibly helpful."

Specific questions get specific answers. "What's your feedback on working with me?" gets you "You're great to work with." "What specifically did I do on the authentication redesign that made it work?" gets you something you can actually put in a packet.

**Choose your witnesses strategically.** You want a mix of:
- Engineers who worked closely with you on your most impactful projects (they have specific, credible evidence)
- Engineers from *other* teams you collaborated with (they demonstrate cross-team scope)
- A senior engineer or Staff+ who can speak to your technical judgment
- Optionally: a product manager or designer who can speak to your collaboration and communication

**Prepare them.** Share the level expectations for the role you are targeting. "Here is what the Staff Engineer level expects at our company. As you write feedback, anything you can share about times you saw me operating at that scope would be really helpful."

**Reciprocate.** If someone writes you a recommendation, offer to write one for them. Keep a mental note of who helped you when you needed it, and pay it back without being asked.

**The quota.** Aim for 3-5 pieces of feedback. More is not always better — 5 strong, specific quotes outperform 10 generic ones. Quality over quantity applies here too.

### Common Promotion Blockers

**"You're great at X but we need to see Y."** This is the most common blocker. You might be the best coder on the team but have not demonstrated cross-team influence. Or you are great at design but have not shown you can execute under pressure. Identify the gap early — ask your manager directly: "What is the one thing that would make my promotion case airtight?" Get a specific answer, not a vague "just keep doing what you're doing."

**"There's no opportunity at that level."** Sometimes your current team does not have problems that require Staff-level work. Options: create the opportunity by identifying a cross-team problem that needs solving, or transfer to a team that has Staff-level problems. Do not wait years hoping the opportunity appears. It is your responsibility to find or create the scope, not your manager's.

**"The project didn't ship."** Canceled projects are not wasted promotion evidence. Document what you delivered before cancellation, the technical decisions you made, the learnings, and the partial value. A well-run project that got canceled due to strategy changes still demonstrates your capabilities. Frame it: "I drove the design and alignment phase of [Project], which was deprioritized due to business reasons. The work produced [specific artifacts] that [teams/engineers] are using as the foundation for [follow-on work]."

**"I don't have metrics."** Not every impact is quantifiable. "Led the migration of 3 teams from monolith to microservices, establishing patterns now used by 12 teams" is compelling even without a latency number. Combine quantitative and qualitative evidence. Quantitative evidence is stronger when you have it — but it is better to have a clear qualitative impact story than a weak quantitative number.

**"I'm being compared to someone who has been at the company longer."** Promotion committees compare you against the level expectations, not against other candidates. If you are meeting the expectations for the next level, that should be sufficient. If you are being told otherwise, that is a management problem, not an evidence problem — and worth addressing directly.

### Working With Your Manager

Your manager is your primary ally in the promotion process (or should be). Have explicit promotion conversations:

- **Quarterly:** "Am I on track for promotion to [level]? What gaps do you see?"
- **After major projects:** "How would this project land in a promotion discussion?"
- **When feedback comes:** "How does this feedback relate to [target level] expectations?"

If your manager is not supportive of your promotion and you believe you have the evidence, consider whether you have the right manager — not all managers are good at (or interested in) advocating for their reports. But before concluding this, make sure you have actually asked for explicit advocacy. Many managers think "be supportive in principle" and "actively advocate in the promotion committee" are the same thing. They are not. Ask explicitly: "Will you actively advocate for my promotion in the committee meeting?"

### Self-Nomination

In many companies, especially larger ones, **you** must drive your own promotion. Your manager may be supportive but will not do the work for you. This means:

- You write the promotion packet (your manager reviews it)
- You collect peer feedback (your manager supplements it)
- You identify the evidence (your manager validates it)
- You set the timeline (your manager confirms feasibility)

Do not assume your manager is tracking your accomplishments with the same fidelity you are. They have 5-10 other reports and their own career to manage. Their mental model of your work is built from the things you make visible to them — which is another reason the brag document (next section) is not optional.

### The Promotion Timeline

Work backwards from when you want to be promoted:

- **T-12 months:** Discuss target level expectations with your manager. Get explicit agreement on what "operating at that level" looks like for you specifically.
- **T-9 months:** Identify and create opportunities to operate at that level. Start documenting everything in your brag document.
- **T-6 months:** Do a dry run with your manager. "Here is my draft promotion case. What are the gaps?"
- **T-3 months:** Fill the gaps. Collect peer feedback. Finalize the packet.
- **T-0:** Submit (or have your manager submit) in the promotion cycle.

This timeline assumes a company with a formal promotion cycle. If your company has continuous promotions, the timeline compresses — but the principle is the same. Be operating at the target level for a sustained period before you make the case.

## 5. The Brag Document

The brag document (a term popularized by Julia Evans) is the single most valuable career tool most engineers are not using. It is a running record of everything you accomplish, updated regularly.

I know what you are thinking: "I do not have time for this." And I know you are wrong. Here is the math. You spend 10 minutes per week on the brag document. That is 520 minutes per year — under 9 hours. In exchange, your performance reviews take half as long and are twice as good, your promotion packet writes itself, and you can answer "tell me about a time when..." questions in interviews without fumbling.

The better question is: can you afford *not* to?

### Why You Need One

You will forget 80% of what you did by the time performance reviews come around. The human brain is not built to remember six months of incremental work. Without a brag document, your self-review will disproportionately reflect the last 2-3 weeks, and you will undersell yourself.

Here is the specific failure mode: it is December, performance review time. You are trying to write your self-assessment. You remember the big feature you shipped in November. You remember the incident you handled last month. You have a vague sense that you did some "good work" in Q1 but you cannot remember specifically what. You get a mediocre review not because your performance was mediocre but because your recall was.

I have seen this cost engineers meaningful raises and promotion timelines. The brag document is 10 minutes a week of prevention against a very expensive failure mode.

### Template

```markdown
# Brag Document — [Your Name]

Last updated: [Date]

## Current Goals
- [Goal 1 for this cycle/quarter]
- [Goal 2]
- [Goal 3]

---

## [Year] — [Q1/Q2/Q3/Q4] (or use months)

### Projects Delivered

**[Project Name]** — [One-line summary]
- What I did: [Specifics]
- Impact: [Metrics or qualitative outcomes]
- Scope: [Team / cross-team / org-wide]
- Challenges: [What was hard about this]
- Date: [When it shipped]

### Technical Decisions & Designs

- [Date]: Wrote design doc for [X], adopted by [teams].
  Key decision: [what you chose and why]
- [Date]: Led architecture review for [X].
  Outcome: [what changed as a result]

### Mentoring & Growing Others

- Mentoring [Name]: [What you focused on, what progress they made]
- Onboarded [Name]: [How you helped them ramp up]
- Ran [workshop/talk] on [topic] for [audience]

### Process & Culture Improvements

- Created [tool/process/document] that [impact]
- Improved [workflow] resulting in [outcome]

### Cross-Team Collaboration

- Worked with [Team X] on [what], resulting in [outcome]
- Represented our team in [initiative/working group]

### Writing & Communication

- Published [blog post/RFC/ADR] on [topic]
- Presented [talk] at [venue]
- Wrote [documentation] used by [who]

### Incident Response

- Responded to [incident]: [your role, outcome, follow-up]

### Feedback Received

- "[Quote]" — [Name, context]
- "[Quote]" — [Name, context]

### Miscellaneous

- [Anything else that does not fit above but you want to remember]

---

## [Previous Quarter]
[Same structure]
```

### How to Maintain It

**Option A: Weekly ritual (recommended).** Every Friday, spend 10 minutes adding to your brag document. What did you do this week? What decisions did you make? Who did you help? Make it a calendar event. Protect it. It is the highest ROI 10 minutes of your week.

**Option B: Bi-weekly.** Every other Friday, spend 15-20 minutes. You will forget more with this cadence, but it is still vastly better than nothing.

**Option C: Event-driven.** Add entries immediately after significant events: shipping a project, writing a design doc, getting positive feedback, handling an incident. Set a calendar reminder to review and fill gaps monthly.

Pick the option you will actually do. A brag document you write consistently is infinitely more valuable than one you write perfectly twice a year.

### The Art of Writing Brag Document Entries

The quality of your brag document entries matters enormously. The difference between a useful entry and a useless one is specificity and completeness. Here is the difference:

**Bad entry:**
```
- Helped with the database migration
```

**Good entry:**
```
- Led the Postgres 13 → 15 migration for the user database (2TB, 12 critical services).
  Designed zero-downtime cutover strategy using logical replication.
  Unblocked the security team's compliance work. Shipped on schedule.
  [Ali Chen] and [Sam Rivera] from the data team can speak to the cross-team coordination.
```

The good entry has: what you did (led, not "helped"), specific scope (which database, how big), why it was hard (zero downtime requirement), who benefited (security team's compliance work), and witnesses. When you are writing your promotion packet 8 months from now, the good entry is immediately usable. The bad entry is useless.

Here are more before/after examples to calibrate your writing:

**Technical decision:**

Bad: `Made architectural decisions for the new feature`

Good: `Chose event-driven architecture over direct service calls for the notification system.
Wrote ADR-42 documenting the decision and tradeoffs.
Alternative was synchronous calls — I argued for events based on reliability and decoupling.
Decision reduced coupling between 6 services and improved notification delivery reliability from ~94% to ~99.7%.`

**Mentoring:**

Bad: `Mentored junior engineers`

Good: `Mentoring [Jordan Kim] on system design over 3 months.
Focused on: breaking problems into components, API design, and writing clear design docs.
Result: Jordan wrote their first design doc solo (Notification Service v2) and got strong feedback from the team.
They credited this work in their mid-year self-review as their biggest growth area.`

**Cross-team work:**

Bad: `Worked with other teams on platform stuff`

Good: `Led alignment between the Infra, Data, and ML teams on shared Kafka topic naming standards.
Previous situation: 3 incompatible naming conventions causing consumer bugs and onboarding confusion.
Wrote the proposal, ran 3 working sessions, reached consensus.
Standard adopted by all 3 teams. Estimate saved ~1 sprint of per-team integration debugging.`

The pattern: **action + scope + why it was hard + outcome + witness or artifact**. Write every entry with enough detail that someone who does not know the context can understand what you did and why it mattered.

### Tagging for Promotion Readiness

Add a simple tagging system to your brag document to make it easier to pull promotion evidence when you need it:

```markdown
**[Postgres Migration]** `#scope:cross-team` `#impact:reliability` `#level:staff`
```

Use these tags:
- `#scope:team` / `#scope:cross-team` / `#scope:org-wide`
- `#impact:reliability` / `#impact:revenue` / `#impact:dx` / `#impact:quality`
- `#level:senior` / `#level:staff` — what level does this demonstrate?
- `#witness:Ali` — who can speak to this work?

When it is promotion time, filter for `#level:staff` and `#scope:cross-team`. Those entries are your packet. The others are your backup.

This sounds like extra work. It is about 5 extra seconds per entry and saves you hours when you are assembling the packet under pressure.

### When to Use It

- **Performance reviews:** Your brag document is the raw material for your self-review. You will write a better review in half the time. Pull directly from it; do not rewrite from memory.
- **Promotion packets:** Your strongest evidence lives here. Build the packet by curating entries from the document, not by trying to remember what you did.
- **1:1s with your manager:** Reference specific accomplishments when discussing your trajectory. "Last quarter I led the database migration, onboarded two engineers, and wrote the design doc for the caching layer — I want to make sure that's reflected in how you think about my performance."
- **Resume updates:** When it is time to update your resume, you have a detailed record to draw from instead of trying to remember what you did two years ago. (See Ch 27 on technical writing — the same principles of specific, impact-driven language apply to your resume.)
- **Job interviews:** Behavioral interview questions ("Tell me about a time when...") are trivially easy when you have a brag document full of concrete examples. You are not trying to remember something impressive from the last three years. You are choosing which impressive thing to share.

### Share It With Your Manager

This is underused. Share your brag document with your manager — not the whole thing, but a quarterly summary. Most managers will appreciate it. The ones who do not need it because they are already tracking your work that carefully are rare.

When you share it, frame it: "Here is a summary of what I worked on this quarter. I want to make sure you have full context when you are thinking about my review and trajectory. What questions do you have or what would you add?"

This does two things: it ensures your manager's mental model of your work is accurate, and it puts your manager on record as understanding your contributions — which matters when they are in the promotion committee.

## 6. Influence Without Authority

Staff+ engineers, Tech Leads without management authority, and anyone who wants to drive change across an organization needs to master influence without authority. You cannot mandate adoption of your ideas — you have to earn it.

This is the skill that most often separates the engineers who get to Staff and the ones who stay Senior indefinitely. It is also the skill that is least often explicitly taught. If you are frustrated because you have "the right answer" that people are not adopting, this section is for you.

### The Fundamental Insight

Authority is a shortcut to influence, not a substitute for it. When a manager tells their team to do something, the team does it — or at least tries. When an engineer with no direct reports proposes something, nothing happens automatically. The proposal has to earn its way into action through persuasion, evidence, and relationship.

This sounds like a disadvantage. It is actually an advantage in the long run. Influence you earn is more durable than authority you are granted. When you change someone's mind with evidence and logic, they become an advocate for the idea. When you mandate something through authority, you just get compliance — and compliance without belief is fragile.

### How to Build Influence

**Write the document nobody else wrote.** When there is a problem everyone complains about but nobody addresses, write the RFC, the design doc, or the ADR. The person who frames the problem controls the conversation. Your proposal does not have to be perfect — it has to be specific enough that people can react to it. Vague complaints stay complaints. Written proposals become decisions.

See Ch 27 for how to write technical documents that actually get read and acted on. The writing skill and the influence skill are deeply intertwined. Engineers who write clearly and persuasively have disproportionate influence relative to their title.

**Build the prototype that proves the idea works.** A working prototype is worth a thousand slides. If you believe the team should adopt a new testing framework, do not write a proposal — build a proof of concept that shows it working in your codebase with real tests. Make it easy for others to try. Concrete demonstrations overcome skepticism that abstract proposals cannot.

**Be the person who fixes things.** Credibility comes from demonstrated competence. Fix the flaky test suite. Clean up the deployment pipeline. Resolve the tech debt that has been annoying everyone. These actions build a reputation that makes people trust your judgment on bigger decisions. You cannot skip this step with clever arguments. Credibility is earned through actions, not words.

**Build relationships across teams.** Know who the key engineers are on adjacent teams. Understand what they are working on and what their pain points are. When you need buy-in for a cross-team initiative, it helps enormously to already have relationships with the people you need to influence. Cold outreach for alignment is hard. Continuing a conversation with someone you have helped before is easy.

**Teach and mentor.** Teaching is the most scalable form of influence. When you run a workshop, write a guide, or mentor someone, you multiply your impact. People who learn from you naturally adopt your approaches and advocate for your ideas. One well-received internal talk on a technical topic can shape how dozens of engineers approach that topic for years.

**Write documentation.** The person who writes the docs shapes how people think about a system. If you want to influence how a service is used, write the definitive guide to it. If you want to change how the team approaches testing, write the testing philosophy doc. Docs are async influence that works while you sleep.

### The "Earn, Don't Claim" Principle

Influence follows demonstrated competence, not declared authority. Saying "I am the technical lead on this" means nothing if people do not trust your judgment. Instead:

- Ship things that work
- Give feedback that makes code better, not just different
- Propose solutions that account for constraints others raise
- Follow through on every commitment, every time
- Credit others publicly and generously

Over time, people will naturally defer to your opinion on topics where you have earned credibility. This is more durable than any title. I have seen Engineers (no "Staff" prefix) who had more influence over technical direction than the Principals at their company because they had spent years earning it. Title is a lagging indicator of influence, not a leading one.

### Navigating Disagreements

**Disagree and commit.** When a decision goes against your recommendation, commit fully to making it work. Do not sabotage it or say "I told you so" when problems arise. Your willingness to disagree openly and then commit fully builds enormous trust. It tells people: "This person's objections are in good faith, not about protecting their ego."

**Know when to escalate.** Most disagreements should be resolved between the people involved. Escalate only when: the stakes are genuinely high, you have tried to resolve it directly, and the disagreement reflects a fundamental difference in direction (not just preference). Escalating over stylistic disagreements burns political capital you need for the real fights.

**Pick your battles.** You have a finite amount of political capital. Spend it on things that genuinely matter. If you fight every decision, people stop listening. If you only push back on things that truly matter, people take your objections seriously. The engineer who objects to everything has no more influence than the engineer who objects to nothing — except the latter is more pleasant to work with.

**Separate the technical from the personal.** The hardest part of influence work is disagreeing with someone without it becoming personal. "I think this approach will have scalability problems" is a technical statement. "You always favor this kind of solution without thinking through the long-term implications" is a personal attack. The first invites a technical discussion. The second invites a defensive response. Frame everything as being about the system, not the person.

### Building Allies

Find people across the organization who share your goals. If you care about testing quality, find the other engineers who do too. If you want to improve the developer experience, find allies on the platform team. Aligned interests create natural coalitions that are more effective than any individual advocate.

The most effective large-scale change I have seen at engineering organizations almost always follows the same pattern: one or two engineers identify a problem, build a small coalition of engineers who agree it is real, create a concrete proposal and prototype, demonstrate results in one team, and use that result to expand. No top-down mandate. No big-bang rollout. Just patient coalition-building and demonstrated results.

### Influence Patterns That Actually Work

Let me be concrete about what influence-building looks like in practice. These are approaches that actually work in engineering organizations — not in theory, but in real teams.

**The "I already fixed it" move.** Instead of proposing a change and asking for approval, make the improvement in a small, reversible way and demonstrate it. "I noticed our API error messages were hard to debug. I updated them in the user service as an experiment — here is the before/after. Should we do this everywhere?" This bypasses the approval cycle for small changes and lets your work speak for itself.

**The "What do you think?" move.** Before publishing a design doc or proposal, share a draft with 2-3 people who will be affected. "I am thinking about [X] — does this make sense from your team's perspective? What am I missing?" This converts potential blockers into co-authors. By the time you publish, the people who could object have already shaped the document. Their fingerprints are on it. They are invested in its success.

**The "I already did the research" move.** When a decision is coming up that you care about, do the research before anyone asks. "I know we will have to decide on a database for the new service. I spent a few hours comparing Postgres, MySQL, and CockroachDB for our use case — here is what I found." Showing up prepared with data and recommendations, before the meeting, positions you as the technical authority on that decision.

**The "This will help your team too" move.** When pitching a cross-team initiative, lead with their benefit, not yours. "I am proposing a shared authentication library. Here is what it would mean for your team: you would not have to maintain [X], you would get [Y] for free, and your setup time for new services goes from [Z] to [less]." Self-interest is not inherently wrong, but framing it as mutual benefit gets you further.

**The "Let me write that up" move.** When a decision is made verbally in a meeting, offer to write the ADR or decision doc. "I will capture that decision and the rationale — I can have a draft by end of day." The person who writes the history shapes the history. This is also genuine value — most teams are terrible at documenting decisions, and engineers who do it well earn genuine appreciation.

### The Influence Inventory

Once a quarter, take stock of your influence:

- On which technical topics do people seek my opinion without being asked?
- Which teams have I built relationships with beyond my immediate team?
- What proposals have I made in the last quarter that were adopted?
- What proposals were not adopted? Do I understand why?
- Who outside my team would say I had a positive impact on their work this quarter?

If you cannot answer these questions with specifics, your influence is thinner than it needs to be for Staff-level work. Build it deliberately.

The influencer who cannot name specific outcomes is like the engineer who claims to "improve performance" without a before/after benchmark. Show your work.

## 7. Navigating Reorgs and Layoffs

Organizational change is not a bug — it is a feature of working at any company that is growing, shrinking, or adapting to market conditions. Expect a significant reorganization every 12-18 months at large companies, and prepare accordingly.

Most engineers treat reorgs like weather — something that happens to them that they have no control over. This is partially true. You cannot control the reorg. You can absolutely control your position going into it and your response to it.

### How to Survive a Reorg

**Have a clear narrative about your value.** When reorgs happen, people make quick decisions about where individuals fit. Leadership is often pattern-matching against their mental model of each person's strengths and specialty. If you can articulate "I am the person who [specific value you provide]" in one sentence, you are more likely to land in a good position. If the answer is "I'm a good engineer who works hard," you are going to land wherever is convenient, not wherever is best for you.

**Build relationships outside your immediate team.** If your entire network is on your current team, you are vulnerable when that team is restructured. Build genuine relationships with engineers, managers, and leaders across the organization. These relationships are your safety net. They are also the mechanism by which you find out about reorgs before they are announced — which gives you time to prepare.

**Stay visible.** People who are known across the organization have more options during a reorg. This does not mean self-promotion — it means doing impactful work and making sure the right people know about it through design docs, presentations, and cross-team collaboration. If your skip-level does not know your name or what you work on, you are invisible when decisions are being made.

**Be adaptable.** The engineers who navigate reorgs best are the ones who can say "this is not what I planned, but I can make it work" genuinely — and who have demonstrated this adaptability before. Being known as someone who makes the best of difficult situations is a strategic asset. Complaining loudly about reorgs — even justified complaints — makes leadership less likely to advocate for you in the next one.

**Know your walk-away number.** Before a reorg happens, decide: what would I need to be true about my new team, role, and manager for me to be genuinely excited? What is the minimum acceptable outcome? What would make me start looking externally? Having these answers in advance means you make the decision with a clear head, not in the emotional fog of "everything just changed."

### Layoff Preparation (Do This Now, Not When Rumors Start)

This is not paranoia. This is routine maintenance on a system that affects your financial security and career trajectory.

**Always have an updated resume.** Update it every quarter when you update your brag document. You should be able to share your resume within 24 hours of any event. If it would take you two weeks to get your resume ready, your brag document is not current enough.

**Maintain your network.** Stay in touch with former colleagues, attend industry events (even occasionally), and be active in professional communities. Your next job is more likely to come from someone you know than from a job board. "Maintaining your network" when you are not looking for a job means staying in touch with people you genuinely like and respect — not transactional check-ins when you need something.

**Keep your skills current.** If your day job uses a legacy stack, spend some time staying current with modern tools and practices. You do not want to be out of date when you need to interview. This does not mean learning every new framework — it means maintaining enough breadth to be competitive in the interview loop at companies you would actually want to work at.

**Save money.** The standard advice of 3-6 months of expenses in savings is especially important in tech, where job searches can take 2-4 months. If you can, target 6 months. Financial runway removes desperation from your decision-making. The best career decisions — including walking away from a bad situation or taking time to find a genuinely good opportunity — require financial cushion.

### The Reorg Positioning Conversation

If you have advance notice of a reorg (or strong signals one is coming), have a proactive conversation with your manager or skip-level. Do not wait to find out where you land.

The script:
> "I am aware there may be some organizational changes coming. I want to be proactive. Here is where I think I add the most value: [specific 2-3 sentence summary of your strengths and what you are best positioned to work on]. I am also flexible — if there are places the organization needs coverage that I have not thought about, I am open to that conversation. What are you thinking about for my role?"

This does three things: it signals self-awareness and initiative, it stakes your territory on terms you control rather than waiting for assignment, and it opens a dialogue before decisions are finalized.

The worst outcome in a reorg is being placed somewhere by default because you did not advocate for where you wanted to be. Most leaders will tell you "we wanted to place you in X but thought you might not be interested" — decisions made by assumption, not information. Eliminate the assumption by having the conversation.

### Signs a Reorg or Layoff Is Coming

No single signal is definitive, but multiple signals together should put you on alert:

- **Hiring freeze:** The company stops all hiring or dramatically slows it
- **Sudden management changes:** Your skip-level or their peers leave or are reassigned
- **"Alignment" meetings:** Leadership schedules mysterious all-hands or strategy sessions with vague agendas
- **Cost-cutting focus:** Sudden attention to cloud costs, tool licenses, and headcount efficiency
- **Leadership departures:** Senior leaders leaving "to pursue other opportunities" in quick succession
- **External signals:** Missed earnings, stock price decline, negative press about the company's business
- **Your manager becomes evasive:** When someone who used to give you straight answers starts hedging, they often know something they cannot say yet

One of these in isolation means nothing. Three or four together means start your network outreach now, before you have to.

### If You Are Laid Off

First: this is not a reflection of your ability or value. Layoffs are business decisions made with spreadsheets, not individual assessments. I have seen exceptional engineers laid off and mediocre ones retained based purely on which team or function was cut. The world is not fair. Your job is to navigate it.

**Negotiate severance.** Even if the company offers a standard package, you can sometimes negotiate more — especially if you have institutional knowledge, if the layoff timing is bad, or if you are willing to help with a transition period. Ask. The worst they say is no.

**File for unemployment immediately.** There is no shame in this. You paid into the system through your taxes. File on day one.

**Update LinkedIn the same day.** Change your status to "Open to Work" (you can make this visible only to recruiters if you prefer). Post a brief message — your network wants to help, but they need to know you are looking.

**Reach out to your network within 48 hours.** Send a short, direct message to former colleagues, mentors, and industry contacts: "I was part of a layoff at [Company]. I am looking for [type of role]. If you know of anything or can make an introduction, I would really appreciate it." Most people genuinely want to help. They just need to know you need it.

**Take a few days before interviewing.** Process the emotional impact before jumping into interviews. A week of rest and reflection will make you a better candidate than panic-applying the day after. This is not the time for a hasty decision — it is the time to be intentional about what you actually want next.

## 8. Switching Jobs Strategically

Job changes are the most powerful lever you have for career growth — in terms of scope, compensation, learning, and trajectory. The data is clear: engineers who switch companies every 3-5 years earn significantly more over their careers than engineers who stay in one place. But switching for the wrong reasons or at the wrong time can set you back just as decisively.

The goal is not to switch often. It is to switch *well*, at the right time, for the right opportunity.

### When to Leave

- **Learning has plateaued.** You are no longer growing technically or professionally, and there is no path to change that on your current team. Warning: make sure you have actually tried to change it. Have you talked to your manager about a new project? Tried to transfer teams? Sometimes the plateau is a team problem, not a company problem.
- **No growth path.** The next level up is blocked (no headcount, no scope, no supportive manager) and lateral moves within the company do not solve it. "No headcount for Staff" is a legitimate blocker. But if your company has ten Staff engineers and you are not one of them, the headcount argument is not the real issue.
- **Values misalignment.** The company's direction, culture, or ethics conflict with your own in ways that are not going to change. This is the most legitimate reason to leave immediately. Do not stay somewhere whose direction you cannot support.
- **Compensation significantly below market.** If you are 20%+ below market and internal adjustments cannot close the gap, the market is telling you something. One or two job offers give you real data on where you are valued. (Note: "market rate" is not a single number — it varies by company type, location, and specialization. Use levels.fyi and get real offers before concluding you are underpaid.)
- **Toxic culture.** Sustained toxicity — not a bad quarter, but a pattern of poor management, burnout culture, or hostile environment — is a valid reason to leave quickly. Your health and psychological safety are worth more than any job.

### When NOT to Leave

- **You are bored.** Try switching teams or projects first. Boredom often means you need a new challenge, not a new company. The grass is often greener — until you realize the new company has different but equally frustrating problems.
- **You had one bad quarter.** Every job has rough patches. One bad project, one difficult manager interaction, or one missed promotion is not a pattern. If you leave at the first difficulty, you never develop the resilience to navigate hard situations — and hard situations happen everywhere.
- **You have not been there a year.** Short stints raise questions in interviews. The exception: truly toxic or unethical environments, or layoff-driven departures. "I was laid off" requires no explanation. "I left after 8 months" requires a very good one.
- **You are chasing a title.** A Staff Engineer title at an unknown company is worth less in your next job search than a strong Senior track record at a well-regarded one. Optimize for learning, impact, and reputation — the compensation follows.

### Job Search Timeline

Expect the full process to take 2-4 months from decision to start date. Plan accordingly.

- **Weeks 1-2:** Update resume (pull from brag document), identify target companies (role fit, growth trajectory, compensation range, culture), start reaching out to network connections at target companies
- **Weeks 3-6:** Apply (both through network and cold applications), do recruiter screens, start interview preparation in parallel
- **Weeks 6-10:** On-site interviews (typically 2-4 companies in parallel — time these to get overlapping offers, which gives you negotiating leverage)
- **Weeks 10-14:** Offers, negotiation, decision, and notice period

The key leverage insight: run multiple processes in parallel so you have competing offers at the same time. A single offer gives you nothing to negotiate with. Two or three offers give you real leverage. To accomplish this, you need to move processes at similar speeds — which requires coordinating timelines with recruiters.

**To slow down a process:** "I am very interested, but I have some competing commitments over the next two weeks. Would it be possible to schedule the onsite for [date]?"

**To speed up a process:** "I have another process that is moving quickly and I expect an offer within two weeks. I am very excited about [Company] and would love to be in a position to consider both. Is there any way to accelerate the timeline on my side?"

Recruiters deal with this constantly. It is not rude or manipulative — it is the normal mechanics of a candidate running a real job search. Most recruiters will work with you to align timelines when you ask professionally.

### Resume for Engineers

Your resume should focus on **impact**, not responsibilities. Nobody cares what you were responsible for. They care what you actually accomplished.

**Bad:** "Worked on performance improvements for the search service."

**Good:** "Reduced p99 search latency from 2s to 200ms by implementing a multi-tier caching layer, improving conversion rate by 3% ($2M annual revenue impact)."

**Bad:** "Responsible for the payments microservice."

**Good:** "Designed and built the payments service handling $50M/month in transactions with 99.99% uptime, reducing payment failures by 40%."

Structure each bullet as: **[Action verb] [what you did] [quantified impact]**.

The brag document is your source material. Every entry you have been writing all year maps to a resume bullet. If you have been writing good brag document entries, your resume update is mostly editing, not writing.

See Ch 27 for the writing principles that apply here. The same rules — be specific, use active voice, lead with the impact — apply to resumes as to technical writing.

### Resume Structure for Senior+ Engineers

The conventional resume advice ("one page!", "keep it brief!") applies to early-career engineers. For Senior+ engineers, two pages is fine. What matters is that every line earns its place.


The key structural decisions:

**Lead with the impact bullet, not the responsibility description.** "Led migration of 15 microservices" is your impact. The "what I was responsible for" context is implicit.

**Include scope signals explicitly.** "org-wide," "8 engineering teams," "cross-team adoption" — these tell the reader your work was Staff+ scope without you having to claim it directly.

**Trim ruthlessly below the fold.** Your last 2 years should have the most detail. Work 5+ years ago can be 2-3 bullets max. Work 10+ years ago can be a single line.

**Do not list technologies as bullet points.** "Worked with Kubernetes" is not an accomplishment. "Established Kubernetes deployment patterns adopted by 8 teams" is.

### Evaluating Offers

Most engineers evaluate job offers by looking at base salary and nothing else. This is a mistake that can cost you hundreds of thousands of dollars over a career.

Rank these factors by your current priorities (they change over time — what you care about at 25 is different from what you care about at 35):

1. **Total compensation:** Base salary + equity + annual bonus + signing bonus. Do not compare base salary alone — equity can be 30-60% of total comp at senior levels. At a pre-IPO company, equity upside can dwarf everything else. At a post-IPO company, RSUs are just delayed cash — model them as such.
2. **Team and manager:** A great manager at an average company beats an average manager at a great company. Ask to meet the team before accepting. Ask the team what their biggest challenges are. Ask the manager what happened to the last person in this role.
3. **Growth opportunity:** Will this role stretch you? Will you learn new things? Is there a path to the next level? If you are a Senior looking to get to Staff, does this company have a Staff level? What does it take to get there?
4. **Work-life balance:** What are the actual working hours? What is the on-call burden? What is the PTO culture (not policy — culture)? Ask: "When was the last time you took a full week off without checking Slack?" The answer tells you more than the official PTO policy.
5. **Mission alignment:** Do you care about what the company does? This matters more than people think, especially over multi-year tenures. It is much easier to work hard and endure the frustrations of any job when you believe in what you are building.

### A Practical Offer Comparison Framework

When you have multiple offers, spreadsheet them. Seriously. The emotional weight of a great interview loop or a charismatic recruiter can skew your judgment. Numbers do not lie.

```
OFFER COMPARISON

                        Company A    Company B    Company C
Base salary             $190K        $195K        $185K
Target annual bonus     $20K         $0           $25K
Equity annual value*    $60K         $45K         $80K
Signing bonus           $30K         $20K         $10K

YEAR 1 TOTAL            $300K        $260K        $300K
YEAR 4 TOTAL            $290K        $270K        $305K
  (signing usually yr 1 only)

Manager quality         ★★★★☆        ★★★☆☆        ★★★★★
Team quality            ★★★★☆        ★★★★★        ★★★☆☆
Growth potential        ★★★☆☆        ★★★★★        ★★★☆☆
Mission excitement      ★★☆☆☆        ★★★★☆        ★★★★★
Work-life balance       ★★★★☆        ★★★☆☆        ★★★★★

* Equity annual value: for public companies, divide total grant by vesting period
  For pre-IPO: conservative estimate (prefer 0 if uncertain, treat upside as bonus)
```

What this usually reveals: the compensation difference between offers is often smaller than it looks when you model it out over 4 years (the typical vesting cycle). And the qualitative differences — manager, growth, mission — are often the more important long-term factors.

It is easy to optimize for year-one total comp and forget that you will be spending 8-10 hours a day with these people for years. The $10K annual difference in base salary is 6 weeks of vacation. But a great manager versus a bad manager is the difference between a career that thrives and one that stalls.

### Interview Preparation for Senior+ Engineers

Senior and Staff engineer interviews are different from junior and mid-level interviews. The technical bar is still there — you still need to pass the coding and system design rounds — but the behavioral and leadership dimensions become equally weighted.

**What interviewers are actually evaluating at Senior+:**

- *Technical judgment:* Not just "can you solve this problem" but "do you ask the right clarifying questions, consider the right tradeoffs, and explain your reasoning clearly?"
- *Scope and ownership:* "Tell me about the most complex system you've owned." They want to understand what you've actually driven end-to-end, not contributed to.
- *Influence and alignment:* "Tell me about a time you had to drive alignment across teams who disagreed." This is a direct proxy for Staff-level skills.
- *Self-awareness:* "What's the biggest mistake you've made?" How you handle this question reveals your ability to learn from failure and your willingness to be honest.

**Prepare your story bank.** Before interviews, identify 8-10 stories from your career that you can adapt to different behavioral questions. Each story should follow the structure: situation, your specific role, what you did, outcome, what you learned.

Your brag document is the raw material. Go through it and identify your most compelling stories — the ones with the clearest scope, the hardest challenges, and the most concrete outcomes. Practice telling them in 2-3 minutes.

**For system design:** Be explicit about your reasoning. "I would choose X over Y because..." not just "I would use X." Interviewers at senior levels are evaluating how you think, not just what you choose.

**For coding:** You can ask more clarifying questions than you think. "Before I start — let me confirm I understand the constraints. Are we optimizing for time complexity, space complexity, or code readability?" This is a signal of maturity, not indecision.

### Counter-Offers

The conventional wisdom is to not accept counter-offers, and it is usually right. The reasons you wanted to leave — the manager, the culture, the lack of growth — will still be there after the raise. Counter-offers also flag you as a flight risk, which can affect future opportunities internally. Some managers will start managing you out immediately after a counter-offer, because they now know your loyalty is conditional and you have shown your hand.

The exception: if your only reason for leaving was compensation and you genuinely love everything else about the role, a counter-offer can make sense. But be honest with yourself about whether money was really the only factor. It rarely is.

## 9. Negotiation

Negotiation is a skill, not a personality trait. You can learn it, practice it, and get better at it. The single most important rule: **always negotiate**. The worst outcome is they say "this is our best offer," and you are no worse off than before you asked.

Here is a real number to sit with: if you negotiate your salary from $190K to $210K when joining a new company, and that company gives you 4% merit increases annually, by year 5 you will be earning roughly $255K instead of $231K. That is a $24K annual difference from one 20-minute conversation. Over a 10-year career, the difference compounds even further. The math is not subtle.

The engineers I know who are the best negotiators are not aggressive or confrontational. They are simply prepared, clear about what they want, and comfortable with a few moments of silence.

### Why You Must Negotiate

- Companies expect you to negotiate. The initial offer is almost never the best they can do. Recruiters are trained to make the initial offer sound final. It is not.
- A $10K difference in base salary, compounded over a career, is worth hundreds of thousands of dollars.
- Negotiating signals confidence and self-awareness — qualities companies want in senior engineers. A Staff engineer who accepts the first offer signals that they do not know their market value.
- Offers are rarely rescinded for negotiating professionally. If a company rescinds an offer because you asked reasonable questions, they have done you a favor.

### Research Market Rates

Before any negotiation, know the market. Use multiple sources:

- **levels.fyi:** The best source for tech compensation data by company and level. Incredibly detailed.
- **Glassdoor:** Useful for non-tech-giant companies
- **Blind:** Anonymous professional network with compensation threads (filter for noise — a lot of it is exaggeration in both directions)
- **Pragmatic Engineer's compensation data:** Curated and reliable
- **Your network:** Ask trusted peers. This gets easier with seniority. Most engineers who have been in the field 5+ years are willing to share comp ranges if you ask directly.

Walk into any negotiation knowing the market rate for your level at that company size and geography. If they offer below market, you have a factual basis for your counter. If they offer at or above market, you know which components have the most upside.

### What to Negotiate

Think about total compensation, not just base salary:

| Component | Flexibility | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Base salary | Medium | Often has band limits per level |
| Equity/RSUs | High | Especially at pre-IPO companies |
| Signing bonus | High | Easiest component to increase |
| Annual bonus | Low | Usually formulaic |
| Relocation | Medium-High | If applicable |
| Title/Level | Medium | Can significantly affect future comp |
| PTO | Low-Medium | Sometimes negotiable at senior levels |
| Remote flexibility | Medium | Increasingly negotiable |
| Start date | High | Easy win if you need time between jobs |

If base salary is capped at the band maximum, the signing bonus is often the easiest way to get more total first-year compensation. Equity (in RSU refresh grants) is often where the real multi-year compensation flexibility lives.

### Techniques

**Have a competing offer.** This is the single strongest negotiating lever. If you can say "I have an offer from [Company] at [amount]," you shift the dynamic from "what will you accept?" to "what will it take to win you?" Do not bluff this — if they ask for a copy of the offer letter, you will be caught. Get the actual offers.

**Name a range, not a number.** "Based on my research and experience, I am targeting $X to $Y for total compensation." The bottom of your range should be what you actually want. They will anchor to the bottom, so set it accordingly. If you want $200K, do not say "$190-210K." Say "$200-220K."

**Ask for specific things.** Instead of "can you do better?", try: "Is there flexibility on the equity component?" or "Could we explore a higher signing bonus to bridge the gap?" Specific asks get specific answers. Vague asks get vague responses.

**If they cannot move on base, ask about other components.** "I understand the base salary is at band maximum. Could we look at an additional equity grant, a signing bonus, or an accelerated review cycle?" The word "or" is important — you are giving them options to find flexibility wherever it exists.

**Use silence.** After making your ask, stop talking. Silence is uncomfortable, and people often fill it by making concessions. Do not negotiate against yourself. Say what you want, then wait. This is genuinely uncomfortable the first few times. It becomes easier.

**Be enthusiastic and clear about your decision criteria.** "I am very excited about this role. The team sounds exactly right. I want to make sure the compensation is where it needs to be so I can say yes confidently. My goal is $X in total comp." This frames you as someone who wants to join, not someone who is using the offer as leverage for a different job.

### What NOT to Do

**Do not share your current salary.** It anchors the conversation against you. In many jurisdictions, it is illegal for employers to ask. If pressed, redirect: "I would prefer to focus on the value I will bring to this role and what the market rate is. What is the range for this position?"

**Do not accept on the spot.** Always ask for time: "Thank you for this offer. I am very excited about the opportunity. Could I have a few days to review the details?" This gives you time to negotiate thoughtfully and, if applicable, to get other offers to the same stage.

**Do not lie about competing offers.** If you claim to have an offer you do not have, and they call your bluff (or ask for proof, which some companies do), you lose all credibility and possibly the offer.

**Do not be adversarial.** Negotiation is a collaboration to find a package that works for both sides. Frame it that way: "I am very excited about this role. I want to make sure the compensation reflects the value I will bring so I can fully commit." Adversarial negotiation makes your first impression a bad one.

**Do not negotiate over email if you can avoid it.** Phone or video gives you tone of voice, allows for real-time adjustment, and is generally harder to misinterpret. Follow up in writing to confirm what was agreed.

### Get Everything in Writing

Before accepting any offer, get the complete package in writing: base salary, equity (number of shares, vesting schedule, strike price if options), signing bonus, annual bonus target, start date, title, and any special agreements (remote work, review timeline, etc.). Verbal promises are forgotten. Written offers are binding.

Specific things to verify in equity:
- Number of shares (not percentage — dilution changes percentages constantly)
- Vesting schedule (4 years with 1-year cliff is standard, but verify)
- Cliff length (1 year is standard; some companies have longer cliffs)
- Strike price (if options) vs current fair market value
- Whether ISOs or NSOs (for pre-IPO companies — tax treatment differs significantly)
- Acceleration provisions (single trigger, double trigger, or none) on acquisition

Most engineers do not read equity paperwork carefully. This is an expensive mistake, particularly at pre-IPO companies.

### Negotiation Conversation Templates

Knowing the principles is one thing. Having the words ready is another. Here are templates for the most common negotiation moments:

**When you receive the offer:**
> "Thank you — I am genuinely excited about this opportunity and the team. I want to make sure I give this the consideration it deserves. Could I have until [date] to review the details and come back to you?"

Never accept on the spot. The deadline they give you is almost always negotiable.

**When you counter on base salary:**
> "I have done research on this role and level using levels.fyi and conversations with peers, and based on that, I am targeting [X] in base salary. Is there room to move in that direction?"

**When you have a competing offer:**
> "I want to be transparent with you — I have an offer from [Company] at [total comp]. I am genuinely more excited about this role and team, and I would love to make this work. Can you come close to [target number]?"

**When base is capped but you want more:**
> "I understand the base salary band is fixed at this level. Are there other components with more flexibility — an additional equity grant, a higher signing bonus, or an accelerated review cycle?"

**When they say "this is our best offer":**
> "I understand. I want to make sure I am comparing everything accurately. Can you walk me through the equity terms in more detail? And is there any flexibility on the signing bonus to help bridge the gap to [target]?"

"This is our best offer" is often not true. It is a closing technique. Respond with a specific question, not a capitulation.

**When you want to accept but want to negotiate the start date:**
> "I am ready to accept — this is exactly what I was hoping for. One thing: I have [commitments / transition needs / vacation] — would it work to start on [date] instead of [earlier date]?"

Start date is almost always negotiable and costs the company nothing. It is the easiest win in the negotiation.

### After the Negotiation: Staying Prepared

Most engineers negotiate intensely when switching jobs and then ignore their compensation for years. Do not do this.

**Once a year:** Check your total compensation against current market data on levels.fyi. If you are significantly below market, have the conversation with your manager during annual review season: "I have been looking at market data, and I want to make sure we talk about compensation at my upcoming review. Here is what I am seeing." This is not threatening — it is professional.

**Every two years:** Seriously evaluate whether your compensation reflects the market. If internal adjustments are consistently below market, that gap will compound. Sometimes the only way to reset is a job change.

**Track your equity vesting schedule.** Know when your cliff dates are, when grants expire (if options), and what your unvested equity is worth at various valuations. This affects the actual cost of leaving at any given time.

## 10. Building Your Brand

Your professional brand is not about self-promotion. It is about being known for something specific by the people who matter in your field. The best opportunities in your career will come from people who know your work, not from job boards.

Here is the truth about career inflection points: they almost always come from relationships and reputation, not resumes. The reason is simple — the best opportunities are filled before they are posted. A hiring manager who needs a Staff-level distributed systems engineer is going to call three people they know before they post a job. If you want to be one of those calls, you have to be known.

This is not networking in the shallow, transactional sense. It is building a genuine reputation — for technical excellence, for generosity with knowledge, for reliability — in the communities that matter in your field. The "brand building" activities below are just ways to make that reputation visible beyond the people you work with directly.

### Write About What You Are Learning

You do not need to be an expert to write. Some of the most valuable technical blog posts are written by people who just figured something out and are explaining it while it is fresh. In fact, the "expert" perspective often misses the beginner's pain because experts forget what it was like to not know.

Write about:

- Problems you solved and how you solved them (the debugging detective story format works extremely well)
- Comparisons of tools or approaches you evaluated
- Lessons learned from failures or incidents (with appropriate discretion)
- Explanations of concepts you found confusing until you understood them
- Design decisions you made and the tradeoffs you considered

Publish on your own blog, on your company's engineering blog (great for internal and external visibility), or on platforms like dev.to or Medium. Consistency matters more than volume — one good post per month beats a burst of five posts followed by six months of silence.

The connection to Ch 27: the same writing skills that make your design docs effective make your blog posts effective. Lead with the insight. Use specific examples. Explain the tradeoffs. If you are developing your technical writing skills for work, your blog benefits for free.

### Speak at Meetups and Conferences

Start small and build up:

1. **Lightning talks at your company:** 5-minute presentations to your team or engineering org. Zero barrier to entry. Immediate feedback.
2. **Local meetups:** 20-30 minute talks at community events. Most meetup organizers are desperate for speakers. Volunteer, and you will likely get a slot within a month.
3. **Regional conferences:** 30-45 minute talks at mid-size events. Submit a CFP (Call for Proposals). Rejection is normal — most well-regarded conferences accept 5-15% of proposals. Keep submitting.
4. **Major conferences:** Once you have a track record and a compelling topic. Being a known quantity helps here — which is why you start with the previous steps.

The compounding effect of public speaking is real. Each talk gets you comfortable with the next. Each audience becomes people who know your work. A good talk at a well-attended conference can result in recruiter outreach, collaboration opportunities, and peer connections that are worth far more than the speaking slot.

### Build a Portfolio

Quality over quantity. Two or three well-documented, well-crafted projects are worth more than 50 abandoned repositories. For each portfolio project:

- Write a clear README explaining what it does, why you built it, and what problems it solves
- Include a demo (deployed app, screenshots, or video)
- Show clean, well-tested code with thoughtful commit messages
- Explain your technical decisions explicitly — the README should answer "why did you do it this way?"

The portfolio is less important for experienced engineers (where your professional history speaks for itself) and more important early in your career (where you need to demonstrate capability beyond what your job history shows).

### Help Others Publicly

Answer questions on Stack Overflow. Help people in GitHub issues. Participate in relevant Discord or Slack communities. Comment thoughtfully on technical posts. This builds your reputation as someone who is knowledgeable and generous with their time — a combination that attracts great opportunities.

The volume does not matter. A single thoughtful, comprehensive answer to a hard question is worth more than a hundred one-line responses. When you help someone solve a hard problem in a public forum, that exchange is searchable and attributable for years.

### Contribute to Open Source

Open source contributions are one of the most visible forms of professional credibility. See Ch 28 for a detailed guide on doing this effectively. Even small contributions — documentation fixes, bug reports, test improvements — make you visible to maintainers and other contributors.

The goal is not to pad your GitHub profile. It is to contribute meaningfully to projects you actually use and care about. The engineers who have built genuine reputations through open source did so by caring about the project and the community, not by chasing contribution graphs.

### The Long Game

Building a professional brand is a multi-year endeavor. The compounding works like this:

1. **Year 1:** You write a few posts, give a talk or two, contribute to a project. A small number of people in your local community know your name and have associated it with something specific.
2. **Year 2-3:** You are a regular at meetups, you have a handful of well-received posts, and people start reaching out to you for advice or collaboration. Recruiters start finding you rather than the reverse.
3. **Year 4-5:** You are a known quantity in your niche. Recruiters reach out with genuinely interesting opportunities (not spam). Conference organizers invite you to speak. People reference your work in conversations without prompting.
4. **Year 5+:** Opportunities find you. You can choose your next role rather than compete for it. Your reputation precedes you in interviews — the conversation is not "can you do this?" but "how do you think about this?"

The best time to start building your brand was five years ago. The second best time is today. Start with one action this week: write a post, submit a talk proposal, or contribute to an open source project.

### Your Brand Is Your Reputation

Ultimately, your brand is what people say about you when you are not in the room. (See Ch 9 on leadership — the same principle applies.) It is built through:

- **Consistency:** Showing up regularly, delivering reliably, following through on commitments. The engineer who delivers every time they say they will deliver is trusted with more.
- **Quality:** Doing work you are proud of, holding yourself to high standards. Excellent work is memorable. Mediocre work blends into the background.
- **Generosity:** Helping others without expecting immediate returns. The people with the best professional reputations I know are all dramatically generous with their time and knowledge.
- **Authenticity:** Being honest about what you know and what you do not. Saying "I do not know, but here is how I would figure it out" is more credible than pretending expertise you do not have.

You cannot shortcut this with personal branding tricks. There is no substitute for doing great work and being a good colleague over an extended period. The "brand building" activities — writing, speaking, contributing — are amplifiers for the reputation you have already earned through your work. They make the invisible visible. They do not manufacture credibility from nothing.

### Internal Brand vs. External Brand

Most of this section applies to both — but they are different things and worth thinking about separately.

**Your internal brand** is what people at your company think of you. It affects your promotion trajectory, the quality of projects you are assigned to, who wants to work with you, and how leadership thinks about you during reorgs. You build it through the day-to-day work, through writing and documentation, through how you handle incidents and hard projects, and through how you treat people across the organization.

**Your external brand** is what people in the broader engineering community think of you. It affects inbound recruiting, speaking invitations, advisory opportunities, and your next job offer (often before you are looking). You build it through blogging, speaking, open source, and being present in the communities in your specialty.

Most engineers focus on one or the other. The engineers with the most career leverage cultivate both deliberately. The internal brand gets you opportunities within the company. The external brand ensures you always have options outside it.

The minimum viable external brand for an experienced engineer:
- An updated LinkedIn profile that reflects your actual work, not a list of job titles
- A GitHub profile with at least one project you are proud of and can point to
- Presence in one or two communities relevant to your specialty (a Slack workspace, a subreddit, a Discord)

From that base, you can expand based on what you enjoy. If you like writing, blog. If you like talking, speak. If you like building, open source. Do the thing you will sustain, not the thing you feel you should do.

### Managing Your Online Presence

A few practical notes:

**LinkedIn matters more than engineers think.** Recruiters absolutely search LinkedIn. Keep your titles, dates, and summary updated. Add key projects and skills. Connect with people you work with and respect. You do not need to post constantly — a well-maintained profile that gets monthly updates is enough.

**GitHub is a portfolio, not a trophy case.** A few good, well-documented repos are worth more than dozens of abandoned projects. Pin your best work. Write READMEs that explain why the project exists, not just what it does.

**Be thoughtful about social media.** Technical Twitter/X (or wherever the tech community migrates next) can be useful for network-building and staying current, but it is a significant time investment. If you engage, do it because you enjoy it, not because you think you should. The returns are real but slow and uneven.

**Write for your future self.** The best technical posts are ones you would have wanted to read when you were figuring out the problem. Imagine a future version of you — or a current colleague — encountering the same issue. What would have saved them 4 hours? Write that.

## 11. Managing Up: Your Manager Relationship as a Career Lever

Your manager is the single most important factor in your career trajectory at any given company. Not your talent, not your work ethic, not the quality of your code — your manager. They control what projects you are assigned, how your work is perceived, whether you are invited into key conversations, and how your promotion case is made. This is not cynical — it is just how organizations work.

Managing this relationship deliberately is one of the highest-leverage career activities you can do. It does not mean being sycophantic or politically maneuvering. It means communicating clearly, setting shared expectations, and making your manager's job easier in ways that also help you.

### What Your Manager Needs From You

Most managers are managing 6-10 people, attending more meetings than they want to, dealing with organizational dynamics they cannot fully share with you, and trying to do their own job on top of all of it. What they need from their reports — and what most reports do not provide consistently — is:

- **Predictability.** If you say something will be done by Thursday, do it by Thursday, or flag early that it will not be. Nothing damages a manager's confidence in a report faster than chronic underdelivery without early warning.
- **Clarity on blockers.** Do not silently sit on a blocker for three days and then announce you are behind. Surface blockers early with a clear statement of what you need: "I am blocked on [X]. I think I need [Y] or [Z] to unblock. Which would you prefer?"
- **Context on your work.** Your manager cannot advocate for you in rooms you are not in if they do not know what you are working on. Make sure they always have an accurate picture.
- **Explicit questions, not vague check-ins.** "Do you have any feedback for me?" is a low-value question. "I am deciding between approach A and approach B for [problem]. Here is my thinking. What would you do?" is high-value. Prepare your 1:1s.

### Running a Good 1:1

Your 1:1 is not a status update meeting — or at least, it should not be. Status updates can go in writing. The 1:1 is where you build the relationship, surface things that are hard to say in a group setting, get coaching, and ensure your manager has an accurate picture of what you are working on and what you need.

A good 1:1 template:

```
STANDING 1:1 AGENDA — [Your Name] / [Manager Name]

Before the meeting (you prepare):
  - What am I working on? (1-2 sentences — high level)
  - What blockers or concerns do I have?
  - What feedback do I want on a recent decision or piece of work?
  - What do I want them to know that they might not? (Cross-team dynamics, risks, etc.)
  - What do I want to ask them? (About their context, org dynamics, my trajectory)

During the meeting:
  [10 min] Your updates and blockers — keep it brief
  [10 min] Coaching/feedback conversation — the high-value part
  [10 min] Their updates, context, and anything they want to cover

After the meeting:
  Note anything you committed to. Do it.
```

Come with an agenda. Send it to your manager before the meeting if possible — it shows respect for their time and sets a collaborative tone. Do not make them drag updates out of you.

### The Career Conversation You Need to Have Quarterly

Most engineers have vague career conversations with their managers — "how am I doing?" "good, keep it up." These conversations are useless. What you need instead is a specific, explicit quarterly conversation about your trajectory.

Here is the structure:

**"Here is what I have done this quarter."** Pull from your brag document. Give them 3-5 specific accomplishments. Make it easy for them to advocate for you.

**"Here is what I am trying to do in the next quarter."** Be specific about which projects you want to work on, what skills you want to develop, and what level of scope you are targeting. Get explicit agreement.

**"Here is where I think I am on the path to [next level]."** Share your self-assessment against the level expectations. Ask: "Do you agree? Where do you see gaps that I do not?"

**"What do I need to do to get more opportunities at [next level] scope?"** The key word is "opportunities." You are asking for access to the work, not for a promotion. This is a much easier ask.

**"What is one thing you would coach me to do differently?"** This is the growth question. A good manager will have a real answer. A bad manager's answer tells you something important too.

### When Your Manager Is Not a Good Advocate

Sometimes you have a manager who is not helping your career — not because they are malicious, but because they are stretched too thin, do not understand the promotion process, are not good at advocacy, or have a misaligned picture of your performance.

Signs your manager is not advocating for you effectively:

- You have had three quarterly conversations about your promotion and nothing has changed
- You are getting promoted around — peers are advancing and you are not, despite comparable work
- You do not know what your manager says about you in conversations you are not in
- Your manager cannot tell you specifically what you need to do to get promoted
- Your skip-level does not know who you are

What to do:

**Make your work visible to your skip-level.** This is not going around your manager — it is building the organizational relationships that every Staff+ engineer has. Send your design docs to a broader distribution. Present in org-wide engineering forums. Have genuine technical conversations with your skip-level when they come up naturally.

**Have the direct conversation.** "I want to make sure I am getting the support I need to get to [next level]. Can we talk about how you are thinking about advocating for me?" A good manager will appreciate this directness. A bad manager's reaction tells you what you need to know.

**Build your external promotion case.** Make sure your skip-level and other engineering leaders have direct experience of your work, not just your manager's summary of it. Volunteer for cross-team projects, lead working groups, present your technical work publicly.

**Consider whether you have the right manager.** Manager quality is not fixed. If you are consistently not getting the support you need, a lateral transfer to a different team (with a different manager) may be more valuable than waiting. The right manager can accelerate your career by years. The wrong manager can stall it indefinitely.

## Putting It All Together

Career engineering is not about optimizing for promotions or maximizing compensation (though those are fine goals). It is about being intentional with the finite time you have — making conscious choices about what you are building, who you are building it with, and what it is worth to you.

The system view of your career: inputs (your work, your relationships, your visibility, your skills) produce outputs (opportunities, compensation, scope, satisfaction). Like any system, you cannot optimize what you do not measure, you cannot debug what you do not instrument, and you cannot improve what you do not iterate on.

Most engineers spend zero time maintaining this system. They do excellent work, hope it gets noticed, and feel vaguely frustrated when it does not translate into the recognition or opportunities they expected. The fix is not working harder. It is working more deliberately.

### The Career Maintenance Routine

Here is the minimal routine that makes all of this tractable:

**Weekly (10 minutes):**
- Update your brag document. Non-negotiable. This is the foundation of everything else. Friday afternoons work well — the week is fresh, the next week has not started yet.

**Monthly (20 minutes):**
- Review your current-quarter goals. Are you on track? What risks do you see?
- Check in on relationships outside your immediate team. Is there someone you should reach out to, collaborate with, or simply say thanks to?
- Add anything to your brag document that slipped through the weekly updates.

**Quarterly (1-2 hours):**
- Review your brag document and update your resume. You should be able to share your resume in under an hour.
- Have an explicit career conversation with your manager: "Am I on track for [next level]? What gaps do you see?" Get specific, actionable feedback.
- Assess honestly: am I learning? Am I growing? Am I valued? Am I being treated fairly?
- Check your compensation against market data (levels.fyi, conversations with peers). Even if you are not planning to leave, knowing where you stand is important.
- Write down your goals for the next quarter — specific projects, scope ambitions, skills to develop, and relationships to invest in.

**Annually (half a day):**
- Evaluate your trajectory honestly: is this the right company, team, and role for where I want to be in three years?
- Update your promotion packet (if you are actively pursuing a promotion). At minimum, keep a draft that is 3-6 months from being complete.
- Set career goals for the next year — not vague ones ("get better at system design") but specific, falsifiable ones: "Write three cross-team design docs. Get feedback from a Principal or Staff engineer on each. Ship two of them. By Q3."
- Do a relationship audit: who are the 10 most important relationships in my professional network? Are those relationships healthy and reciprocal? Where are the gaps?
- Review your financial position relative to your career runway. Savings, equity vesting, emergency fund. Know your numbers.

**Ongoing:**
- Build relationships across your organization and industry — invest in people you respect, regardless of what they can do for you. The transactional version of this falls apart the first time someone can tell you are only engaging because you want something.
- Write, teach, and share what you learn — even 20% of what you know that others do not is valuable. Write it up. Share it publicly or internally. The discipline of explaining something clearly deepens your own understanding.
- Help others, generously and without a scoreboard. The career karma is real, but do not do it for the karma. Do it because the engineering community is small, and the help you give will always find its way back around.

### The Three-Year Horizon

The most effective career engineers I know all think on a three-year horizon. Not "what do I want my job to be next year?" — that is too short to change trajectory. Not "where do I want to be in 10 years?" — that is too long to act on.

Three years is the right window because:
- It is long enough to require real investment and sustained effort
- It is short enough to have a concrete picture of what success looks like
- Most career changes (switching companies, getting to Staff, building a meaningful external reputation) take 2-3 years of deliberate work

Ask yourself: "Where do I want to be professionally in three years?" Be specific:
- What level?
- What kind of work?
- What domain expertise?
- What kind of company (startup, growth-stage, enterprise)?
- How do I want to spend my time day-to-day?

Then work backwards: what do you need to have done in two years for that three-year goal to be achievable? What in one year? What this quarter?

This is exactly how you would plan a complex engineering project. You start with the desired state and work backwards to the required milestones. Apply the same method to your career.

### The Honest Assessment

Once a year, answer these questions honestly — not the answer you wish were true, but the answer that is actually true:

```
Career Health Check

1. Am I genuinely learning new things, or am I doing the same work I did last year?

2. Do the people around me make me better, or do I feel like the smartest person in the room?
   (Being the smartest person in the room feels good; it is also a sign you need to find a different room.)

3. Is my compensation within 10% of what the market would pay me for comparable work?

4. Do I have a clear path to the next level, or am I just hoping the path will appear?

5. Does my manager know my career goals, my current work, and my strengths?
   Or am I hoping they notice without me having to say anything explicitly?

6. When was the last time I helped someone else grow or solve a hard problem?

7. Do I have documented evidence of my impact, or would I be scrambling if
   I needed to apply for a job tomorrow?

8. Am I proud of what I am building? Does the work matter to me?

9. Is my technical foundation growing or atrophying?
   (Learning one new thing per quarter is a reasonable bar.)

10. Do I have enough financial runway that I could leave this job without panic
    if the situation became unacceptable?
```

If you answered "no" to more than three of these, you have work to do. Not on your technical skills — on the career system around them. The good news: every one of these is fixable with deliberate action over 3-6 months.

### A Note on Patience and Urgency

Career engineering has an interesting temporal duality. You need **urgency** — start the brag document now, not next month; have the career conversation this quarter, not next year; build the relationship with your skip-level before you need something from them. Delay compounds negatively. Every month you wait to start is a month of compound interest you do not collect.

At the same time, you need **patience** — promotion timelines are measured in years, not sprints; reputation is built over decades, not quarters; the best job opportunities often come from relationships you invested in years before you needed them. Impatience leads to job-hopping for marginal gains, to burning bridges by pushing too hard too fast, to measuring progress on a timescale where none is visible.

The engineers who figure this out — urgent on the inputs, patient on the outputs — tend to have the best long-run careers. They are doing the work every week without expecting the reward this week.

### Common Failure Modes

For completeness — here are the patterns I see most often:

**The passive waiter.** Does excellent work, never advocates for themselves, assumes the organization will notice and reward it automatically. Often gets excellent performance reviews and never gets promoted because the promotion case was never made explicitly. Fix: read Sections 4 and 5, start immediately.

**The title chaser.** Optimizes for advancing to the next level rather than for doing excellent work at the current level. Often achieves the promotion and then struggles because the foundation was not there. Fix: build the evidence by doing the work, not by gaming the process.

**The hyperactive networker.** Invests enormous time in visibility and relationships while their technical output suffers. Becomes known as someone who talks about good work rather than does it. Influence based on relationships without the substance to back it up is fragile. Fix: substance first, visibility second.

**The lone genius.** Technically outstanding but unable or unwilling to make their work legible to others. Could write the promotion packet but will not. Could build the relationships but sees them as a waste of time. Often stuck at Senior indefinitely despite exceptional technical skill. Fix: read Section 6 and Ch 27.

**The perpetual planner.** Has a detailed career plan, reviews it quarterly, updates it meticulously — and never actually does anything differently as a result. Planning is not the work. It is the plan for the work. Fix: for every plan, identify the next concrete action and do it within 48 hours.

The engineers who have the best careers are not the ones who are the best at coding (though they are usually very good). They are the ones who are intentional about where they are going, build evidence of their impact, and invest in relationships. They treat their career with the same rigor they apply to their systems: they instrument it, they iterate on it, and they debug it when it goes wrong.

You can do all of this without being political, without being self-promotional, and without compromising your technical depth. It just takes the same discipline you apply to your code — and the same willingness to actually run the system instead of just designing it in your head.

Your career is a system. Optimize it like one.

> **Key Takeaways**
> - The career ladder is a specification document — read it, and understand exactly what "next level" means in concrete terms.
> - Promotions are evidence-based. Build your promotion packet 6-12 months before you want to be promoted, not when you submit it.
> - The brag document is not optional. Ten minutes a week now saves hours of frustration later and is the foundation for every career conversation you will have.
> - Influence without authority is the core Staff+ skill. Earn it through demonstrated competence, not declared intent.
> - Negotiate every offer — always. The worst outcome is "no," and you are no worse off than before you asked.
> - Your manager relationship is your most important career lever. Invest in it deliberately; do not leave it to chance.
